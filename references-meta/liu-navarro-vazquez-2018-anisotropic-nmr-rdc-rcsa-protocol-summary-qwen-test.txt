"Application of anisotropic NMR parameters to the confirmation of molecular structure." *Nature Protocols*, Vol. 14, pp. 217–247. DOI: 10.1038/s41596-018-0091-9. 31 pages. 111 references. This protocol paper targets researchers in small-molecule NMR and natural product structure elucidation. It provides a complete experimental and computational workflow for measuring residual dipolar couplings (RDCs) and residual chemical shift anisotropies (RCSAs) using compressed or stretched polymeric gels. The authors argue that anisotropic parameters serve as independent constraints for validating molecular constitution and configuration, particularly for proton-deficient scaffolds where conventional isotropic data are insufficient. The stance is strictly methodological, presenting reproducible procedures rather than novel theoretical derivations.

The protocol details the synthesis of chloroform-compatible poly(methyl methacrylate) and DMSO-compatible poly(2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate) gels, followed by sample preparation in reversible compression or glass-based stretching devices. RDCs are extracted as differences in 1H–13C total couplings between two alignment states, measured via F2-coupled CLIP–HSQC or F1-based J-resolved/J-scaled HSQC experiments. RCSAs are obtained from 13C chemical shift differences; the stretching method yields data directly, while compression requires post-acquisition isotropic shift correction. Computational modeling proceeds through MMFF94-based Monte Carlo conformer search, DFT geometry optimization at B3LYP/6-31G**, and GIAO-DFT shielding tensor calculation at MPW1PW91/6-31G**. Structural validation employs singular value decomposition to determine a single effective alignment tensor, with Boltzmann-weighted ensemble averaging and Eckart superposition. Agreement is quantified by a Q factor and visualized via experimental versus back-calculated correlation plots.

The workflow is demonstrated on cryptospirolepine (505 Da), retrorsine (351 Da), and estrone (270 Da). Suitable alignment yields one-bond 1H–13C RDCs of 10–30 Hz, with a practical span of 30–50 Hz. sp2 carbon RCSAs typically reach 5–10 Hz on a 600 MHz instrument, while sp3 carbons produce smaller values. Gel quality is monitored via residual 2H quadrupolar couplings of the solvent, ranging from ~3–10 Hz under weak alignment to 30–50 Hz under strong alignment. Correct structural proposals consistently produce Q factors below 0.2 with linear correlations; estrone yields Q = 0.135 (compressed) and Q = 0.123 (stretched), whereas its 13-epi isomer yields Q = 0.354 and Q = 0.396. Sample recovery exceeds 90%. When Q factors are similar across candidates, the QCSA weighting factor or orthogonal alignment media improve differentiation.

The paper builds on foundational work by Luy and coworkers on stretched gels and RCSA measurement, Gil and Griesinger on gel synthesis and alignment devices, and Prestegard on stretching instrumentation. It adopts the SVD alignment tensor formalism of Losonczi and Prestegard and the Q-factor metric of Cornilescu and Bax. The authors position the method alongside CASE programs such as CHEMICS, COCON, LSD, and the Mnova StereoFitter module, and point toward future integration of anisotropic data with isotropic shifts and scalar couplings. References also cover lyotropic liquid crystalline media, long-range RDC measurement, and conformational averaging protocols.

This protocol documents a calibration workflow that correlates DFT-derived shielding tensors and molecular geometries with experimental RCSA and RDC data via SVD alignment tensor fitting. The approach captures the signal of tensor orientation through ensemble-averaged back-calculation, with agreement evaluated via correlation plots and Q factors rather than pointwise match. While the physical observables and target systems differ from the thesis focus on protein structures and geometric kernels, the computational pipeline shares structural parallels with kernel calibration. The protocol does not address protein backbone constraints, ring-current or bond anisotropy kernels, or rank-2 tensor preservation. Its primary relevance lies in the handling of conformational flexibility through single-tensor ensemble fitting and the use of correlation-based validation metrics, which can inform how shielding predictions are benchmarked against experimental tensors in flexible systems.
