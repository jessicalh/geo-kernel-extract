Markwick, P.R.L., Cervantes, C.F., Abel, B.L., Komives, E.A., Blackledge, M. & McCammon, J.A. (2010) "Enhanced Conformational Space Sampling Improves the Prediction of Chemical Shifts in Proteins." Journal of the American Chemical Society 132(4), 1220–1221. DOI: 10.1021/ja9093692. Received 4 November 2009; published web 11 January 2010. 2 pages (JACS Communication), 16 reference entries (several multi-part). UCSD Chemistry/Biochemistry + HHMI (Markwick, Cervantes, Abel, Komives, McCammon); Institut de Biologie Structurale UMR 5075 CEA-CNRS-UJF, Grenoble (Blackledge). Acknowledges Jane Dyson, G. Kroon, and the Scripps Research Institute NMR facility.

A JACS Communication demonstrating that enhanced conformational sampling via Accelerated MD (AMD) gives better chemical-shift prediction than either a static X-ray structure or plain MD, using the ankyrin repeat protein IκBα (residues 67–206) as the principal test case — chosen for its heterogeneous distribution of dynamics, with some regions static and others undergoing slow (ms) conformational exchange. Ubiquitin is used as a counter-example with less dynamical heterogeneity.

Method. Accelerated MD with dual-boost dihedral biasing (Hamelberg-McCammon 2004). Twenty 10-million-step AMD trajectories at twenty increasing torsional acceleration levels, each pre-pruned — the top ~80% of high-energy structures removed, the remaining 20% clustered to extract conformational substates. Controls: five 10 ns plain MD runs and the X-ray reference (PDB 1NFI). Chemical shifts for H^N, N, Cα, Cβ, and C' computed with SHIFTX (Neal-Nip-Zhang-Wishart 2003) — an empirical database-trained predictor, not DFT — and free-energy-weighted over each ensemble. Modified AMBER10.

Results. IκBα cumulative RMSD against experiment: X-ray 8.19 ppm, plain MD 7.44 ppm, optimal AMD 5.92 ppm — a 28% improvement over X-ray and 20% over plain MD. The ¹⁵N channel carries the largest gain (2.89 → 1.84 ppm RMSD). Optimal acceleration parameters [E_b(dih)−V(dih) = 600 kcal/mol, α(dih) = 120 kcal/mol] also gave best agreement with residual dipolar couplings in Markwick's earlier work — a cross-validation against an independent NMR observable. Per-residue analysis shows the shift-prediction improvement coincides with regions of enhanced conformational sampling (lower NH order parameters S²), which coincide with regions of ms-exchange dynamics independently measured by R1/R2/hetNOE relaxation (Cervantes et al. 2009). Ubiquitin, lacking heterogeneous dynamics, shows smaller improvement — the principle holds but the gain scales with dynamical complexity. Key observation: "No individual molecular ensemble obtained at the optimal acceleration level gave a chemical shift prediction as good as the trajectory-averaged result" — the averaging is essential, not a lucky-frame artifact.

Implications for our work. Stage 2 ancestor on real proteins, complementing de Gortari 2010's solid-state-tripeptide demonstration. Three substantive differences from our thesis: (i) the chemical-shift predictor is SHIFTX — empirical, database-trained — rather than DFT. What Markwick et al. validate is the averaging principle for an empirical predictor; our DFT approach is physics-grounded and absolute-referenced, so the principle's applicability needs independent confirmation for us (and de Gortari 2010 provides exactly that for a small peptide). (ii) Their MD is Accelerated MD with a dihedral bias that distorts the Boltzmann distribution and requires reweighting; our plain 25 ns AMBER ff14SB is simpler but samples less aggressively. (iii) Their output is scalar shifts for five backbone nuclei; ours is rank-2 tensors. Four transferable findings: (a) ¹⁵N is the largest-magnitude reporter of dynamics effects in protein backbones (2.89 → 1.84 ppm is the biggest gain), consistent with our Stage 1 observation that backbone N is the hardest element-channel and the hardness is plausibly dynamics-linked. (b) Regions of shift-improvement coincide with regions of ms-exchange dynamics measured independently by R1/R2 relaxation — a cross-validation pattern our Stage 2 analysis should recover if our kernels are picking up physics rather than static-structure artefacts. (c) The "no single frame matches the trajectory average" finding justifies averaging over frames rather than selecting a representative, underwriting our 600-frame averaging across 25 ns. (d) Ubiquitin's lower responsiveness to MD averaging suggests Stage 2 validation should include dynamically-heterogeneous systems to maximise signal — the current 10-protein calibration set should be audited for dynamical diversity.
