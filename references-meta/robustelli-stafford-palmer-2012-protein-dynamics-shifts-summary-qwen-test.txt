Robustelli, P.; Stafford, K. A.; Palmer, A. G. III. Interpreting Protein Structural Dynamics from NMR Chemical Shifts. J. Am. Chem. Soc. 2012, 134, 6365–6374. DOI: 10.1021/ja300265w. 10 pages. 51 references. Funded by NSF postdoctoral fellowship (1002684) to P.R., NSF graduate fellowship to K.A.S., and NIH grant GM50291 to A.G.P. Acknowledges N. Trbovic for providing a 100 ns ttRNH trajectory.

This is an empirical research paper evaluating whether dynamically averaged NMR chemical shift predictions from molecular dynamics (MD) simulations improve agreement with experimental values compared to predictions from static X-ray crystal structures. The authors examine two structurally homologous ribonuclease H enzymes, ecRNH (1.5 Å, PDB 2RN2) and ttRNH (2.8 Å, PDB 1RIL), to identify which conformational changes and motional processes drive prediction improvements. The paper argues that MD-averaged chemical shifts serve as sensitive reporters of backbone and side chain torsional fluctuations, aromatic ring positions, hydrogen bond geometries, and side chain χ1 rotamer populations, and that discrepancies between predicted and experimental shifts can flag spurious MD conformations or force field deficiencies.

The study employs five semiempirical chemical shift prediction tools (Sparta+, Shiftx2, Shiftx+, Camshift, ShiftS) to calculate backbone Cα, Cβ, C′, Hα, HN, and N shifts for individual MD snapshots. Trajectories were generated using the Amber99SB and Amber99SB-ILDN force fields in TIP3P water at 300 K, with lengths of 100 ns, 450 ns, and 1 μs, saving snapshots every 4.5 ps. Predicted shifts were linearly averaged across each trajectory. Coordinates underwent a 200-step steepest-descent minimization in the Amber03 force field using the almost-1.0.4 toolkit to standardize hydrogen geometries before prediction. Experimental shifts were rereferenced using Shiftcor. The analysis compares root mean square deviations (rmsd) between predicted and experimental values, and examines per-residue deviations to isolate structural drivers.

Sparta+ rmsd values against experimental shifts decrease for most backbone nuclei when using MD-averaged predictions versus X-ray structures. For ttRNH, rmsd reductions range from 0.09 ppm (HN, Hα) to 0.53 ppm (N). For ecRNH, reductions are 0.04 ppm (Cα), 0.03 ppm (HN), and 0.26 ppm (N), with Hα showing no change (0.25 ppm for both). The reported Sparta+ accuracy on static X-ray databases is 0.49 ppm for HN and 0.94 ppm for Cα. Improvements arise from population-weighted sampling of helix fraying, β-sheet/coil equilibria, and side chain χ1 rotamer distributions. Averaged 1H shifts capture aromatic ring current fluctuations, with ring current parametrizations showing rmsd < 0.1 ppm against benchmark quantum calculations. Discrete conformational corrections, such as the formation of a hydrogen bond at Phe 27 or the stabilization of an N-cap hydrogen bond at Leu 73, also improve agreement. At Leu 73, the trajectory splits into two states: H-bond present yields a shift of ~8.2 ppm and S² = 0.82; H-bond absent yields ~9.2 ppm and S² = 0.60. The experimental shift is 8.0 ppm and S² = 0.84, indicating the H-bond-absent state is spurious. ShiftS produces noisier, multi-modal distributions and higher rmsd due to its quantum fragment-based parametrization, whereas predictors trained on X-ray databases remain sensitive to conformational fluctuations.

The paper builds on established chemical shift prediction methodologies (Sparta+, Shiftx2, Camshift, ShiftS) and MD force field developments (Amber99SB, Amber99SB-ILDN). It references prior work linking chemical shifts to S² order parameters and backbone dihedral populations, as well as studies using shifts to refine force fields. The authors cite ring current parametrization benchmarks, constant pH simulation requirements for active site loops (residues 120–127), and the 4DSPOT predictor, which trains on averaged MD properties rather than static structures. The work positions itself within the broader effort to use NMR observables as restraints for conformational sampling and to validate MD trajectories against solution-phase data.

The paper treats chemical shifts as isotropic scalar averages derived from semiempirical predictors, without addressing shielding tensor components or rank-2 preservation. It does not use DFT-calculated shielding values, nor does it decompose shifts into geometric kernel contributions. The linear averaging of scalar predictions over MD trajectories contrasts with our approach of preserving tensor rank and calibrating geometric kernels against DFT shielding. However, the per-residue analysis of how specific structural motifs (ring currents, hydrogen bonds, χ1 rotamers, backbone torsions) drive prediction deviations aligns with our kernel construction strategy. The identification of spurious conformational states via shift discrepancies supports the use of experimental data to validate trajectory ensembles, a step relevant to our calibration diagnostics. The paper demonstrates that MD-averaged predictions correlate with experimental values, capturing the signal of conformational averaging that static structures miss. It does not directly inform our tensor calibration or kernel parameterization, but it reinforces the principle that solution-relevant conformational ensembles are required to capture the structural signal underlying NMR observables.
