Markley, J. L., Bax, A., Arata, Y., Hilbers, C. W., Kaptein, R., Sykes, B. D., Wright, P. E., & Wüthrich, K. (1998). Recommendations for the presentation of NMR structures of proteins and nucleic acids. Journal of Biomolecular NMR, 12, 1–23. 23 pages. 71 references. Funded by ICSU and CODATA; reprinted with IUPAC permission from Pure and Applied Chemistry 70, 117–142 (1998).

This document is a consensus standardization guideline produced by an IUPAC-IUBMB-IUPAB Inter-Union Task Group. It establishes reporting conventions for NMR-derived three-dimensional structures of proteins and nucleic acids, targeting journal authors, database curators, and structural biologists. The scope covers atom nomenclature, resonance assignment protocols, conformational constraint collection, structure calculation and refinement, validation metrics, and public data deposition. It does not present new experimental measurements or computational methods; rather, it codifies community practices to ensure reproducibility and machine-readable exchange of structural data.

The organizing principles centre on unambiguous atom identification, consistent pseudoatom notation, and standardized torsion angle definitions. For proteins, the 1969 IUPAC-IUB main-chain precedence rule is adopted, with Greek letter designators and slash notation for assignment ambiguities. Pseudoatoms representing unassigned proton groups are designated with Q (general groups) and M (methyl groups) prefixes. Torsion angles are defined by highest-precedence atom pairs spanning rotatable bonds, with explicit conventions for protein backbone (φ, ψ, ω), side chains (χ), and nucleic acid backbones (α through ζ), glycosidic bonds (χ), and sugar puckers (ν0–ν4). Chemical shift referencing is standardized to internal DSS at 0 ppm, with fixed relative frequency (Δ) ratios for indirect referencing of 2H, 13C, 15N, and 31P. Structure reporting requires ensemble statistics, constraint violation summaries, and validation against database-derived stereochemical checks.

The recommendations specify diagnostic numerical thresholds and calibration constants. The Δ values are fixed at 100.000000 (1H), 15.3506088 (2H), 25.1449530 (13C), 10.1329118 (15N), and 40.4808636 (31P). Diagnostic J-coupling ranges for sugar pucker identification are given as 3JH1'H2' ≈ 1 Hz (N-type, P = 0°–18°) and ≈ 7.9 Hz (S-type, P = 144°–162°) in ribose, and ≈ 1.8 Hz and ≈ 10 Hz in deoxyribose. Proline ring pucker torsion angles are tabulated for trans/cis and UP/DOWN conformations (e.g., trans DOWN: χ1 = 18°, χ2 = −29°, χ3 = 17°, χ4 = −4°). Hydrogen bond distance constraints are standardised to ±0.2 Å from crystallographic references. NOE constraints are classified by sequence separation (intraresidue, sequential, medium-range |i−j| < 5, long-range, intermolecular). The paper notes that direct ab initio validation of NMR structures remains unavailable, relying instead on Ramachandran analysis, contact maps, and software suites such as PROCHECK-NMR.

The document builds on earlier IUPAC-IUB nomenclature recommendations (refs 5–8), the 1969 tentative peptide rules (ref 20), and the 1983 polypeptide guidelines (ref 21). It references foundational database infrastructure (PDB, ref 1; BMRB, ref 2; NDB, ref 3), pseudoatom formalisms (Wüthrich et al., ref 30), Karplus-based J-coupling analysis (ref 50), and chemical shift referencing protocols (Wishart et al., ref 42). It points toward future data dictionary development, mmCIF/STAR format compatibility, and ASN.1 exchange standards, citing Ulrich & Markley (ref 71) and international committee efforts for NMR data archiving.

For this thesis, the paper provides the structural reporting baseline required to map geometric features to tensor components without ambiguity. The standardized atom identifiers, pseudoatom conventions, and torsion angle definitions ensure that deposited PDB/BMRB structures can be parsed consistently for kernel construction. The constraint classification and validation metrics contextualise how input structures are annotated and quality-controlled, though our calibration relies on DFT-derived shielding rather than experimental shift scales. The recommendations do not address shielding tensors, ring-current models, or calibration procedures; they serve as a nomenclatural and archival framework that enables reproducible geometric kernel extraction from publicly available protein structures.
