Yang, Z., Chakraborty, M., & White, A. D. (2021). Predicting chemical shifts with graph neural networks. *Chemical Science*, 12, 10802–10809. DOI: 10.1039/d1sc01895g. 8 pages. 73 references. Funded by NSF (1764415, 1751471) and NIH (R35GM137966); computational resources provided by CIRC, University of Rochester.

This research paper presents a graph neural network framework for predicting NMR chemical shifts directly from three-dimensional molecular structures. The authors position the work as a differentiable forward model capable of handling arbitrary molecules, contrasting it with existing protein-specific, non-differentiable empirical models that rely on hand-engineered features. The central argument is that a message-passing GNN trained end-to-end on structural graphs can achieve accuracy comparable to state-of-the-art methods while remaining computationally efficient and compatible with gradient-based molecular simulation.

The model implements a message-passing neural network (enn-s2s architecture) where inputs consist of atom element identities and a molecular graph encoding covalent and spatial neighbor relationships with distances. The architecture comprises an edge feature network, four message-passing layers with residue connections, and three fully connected output layers. Training utilizes three datasets: a large fragment dataset derived from 2,405 proteins (RefDB), a carefully curated protein dataset (SHIFTX), and a metabolome database of 369 organic molecules. The loss function combines root mean squared deviation with a correlation penalty term. Hyperparameters were optimized on a validation split, and training employed Adam optimization with a decaying learning rate schedule and early stopping.

The H-only model captures the signal with an RMSD of 0.459 ppm and a correlation coefficient of 0.781 on the SHIFTX test set. The multielement model trained on all datasets yields RMSDs of 0.527 ppm (H), 0.293 ppm (Ha), and 0.264 ppm (Hr), with correlation coefficients of 0.844 (Ha) and 0.878 (H). The Ha correlation corresponds to an r² of 0.712. The model captures secondary structure dependencies, reproducing the expected downfield shift for β-sheets and upfield shift for α-helices in Ha, and reproduces the upfield shift associated with breaking salt bridges, consistent with prior experimental measurements. Inference requires approximately 5.7 ms per shift on a single GPU.

The paper situates its approach within the broader GNN literature, citing foundational message-passing frameworks (Gilmer et al., Battaglia et al.) and recent surveys (Wu et al., Dwivedi et al.). It compares performance against established NMR prediction tools including ProShift, SPARTA+, ShiftX+, ShiftX2, CamShift, and CS2Backbone. The phenomenological validation references experimental and computational studies on secondary structure effects (Mielke & Krishnan; Avbelj & Baldwin; Szilagyi & Jardetzky) and hydrogen-bond-induced deshielding (White et al.). The work builds on prior applications of GNNs to molecular properties but extends them to macromolecular NMR shifts.

This study demonstrates that differentiable, data-driven forward models can capture structure-to-shift correlations without explicit physical kernels or feature engineering. However, it operates exclusively on scalar chemical shifts and does not preserve rank-2 tensor structure or provide a mechanism for calibration against first-principles shielding values. The reliance on large fragment datasets and the absence of explicit physical constraints contrast with the thesis approach of constructing geometry-based kernels calibrated to DFT. The paper serves as a benchmark for the correlation ceiling of purely neural forward models on scalar shifts, but offers no direct methodological transfer for tensor-preserving kernel construction or DFT-based calibration.
