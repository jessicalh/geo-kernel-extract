Robustelli, P.; Kohlhoff, K.; Cavalli, A.; Vendruscolo, M. "Using NMR Chemical Shifts as Structural Restraints in Molecular Dynamics Simulations of Proteins." Structure 18 (2010) 923-933. DOI: 10.1016/j.str.2010.04.016. 11 pages, 36 references. Subscription (Elsevier).

A methods paper from the Vendruscolo lab introducing CS-MD, a procedure that uses six backbone chemical shifts (1Hα, 13Cα, 13Cβ, 13C', 1HN, 15N) as continuous restraints on molecular dynamics. The audience is the structural-NMR community; the stance is that shifts should slot into restrained-MD pipelines alongside NOEs, J-couplings, and residual dipolar couplings (RDCs), not only into fragment-replacement folding tools such as Cheshire, CS-Rosetta, and CS23D. The paper proposes the apparatus, tests it on twelve proteins, and uses it together with RDCs to characterize a transiently populated excited state.

Shift prediction uses CamShift (Kohlhoff et al. 2009): each backbone shift is a polynomial in interatomic distances — through-space within a 5 Å sphere, neighbouring-backbone, side-chain, dihedral cosine series in φ/ψ/χ1, and a point-dipole ring current of the form (1−3cos²θ)/r³ — with parameters fit on RefDB (Zhang et al. 2003). Differentiability turns the penalty E_CS = α·Σ E_ij into atomic forces. E_ij is a soft-square harmonic well: a flat bottom of width n·ε_j (zero force inside CamShift's expected accuracy ε_j for shift type j), a harmonic core, and a hyperbolic-tangent tail beyond x_0 (4 ppm for 1H, 20 ppm for heavy atoms) that bounds outlier contributions. Simulations ran in ALMOST (open-almost.org) with AMBER03 + generalized-Born implicit solvent, replica exchange across 21 temperatures (270–515 K), 2 fs steps, and SHAKE applied to all bond lengths and angles — angle-SHAKE is required because shift-derived forces can otherwise overwhelm bond-angle terms. With α=1 during MD and α=5 for scoring, eleven of twelve proteins folded from partially denatured starts (3.5–7 Å rmsd) to within 0.8–2.2 Å of their reference structures; unrestrained controls succeeded for one of twelve.

The single CS-MD failure (NE1242, PDB 2jv8, 19% α / 24% β with a large disordered loop) is informative. Removing the chemical-shift restraints on residues 28–58 and 68–73 recovers a fold within 1.4 Å of the reference. CamShift was parameterized on crystallographic loops; in solution those loops sample many conformations and shifts approach random-coil values (De Simone et al. 2009), so a single-conformation restraint there pulls the global fold off. A separate test starting from fully extended GB3 also failed on ns timescales, getting trapped in a non-native hydrophobic core — the authors are explicit that CS-MD refines a roughly correct topology rather than folding ab initio. A head-to-head replica-exchange Monte Carlo run on GB3 with the same penalty rejected over 90% of moves and produced no significant conformational change in equivalent CPU time; the move from MC to MD is the engineering point. Adding RDCs to a CS-MD calculation of the peptide-bound (5%-populated) excited state of the Abp1p SH3 domain produced five lowest-energy structures within 0.51 Å of each other, with Q-factors of 0.34–0.35 on PEG/hexanol RDCs not used as restraints.

This paper is direct upstream of `gadanecz-perczel-2026-edmd-cs-restraints`, which carries the methodology forward sixteen years (eDMD substitutes CS-Rosetta-derived φ/ψ potentials of mean force in place of the AMBER torsion terms; Robustelli 2009 is in that paper's reference chain). CamShift's functional form — point-dipole ring currents, dihedral cos-series, through-space distance polynomials — overlaps directly with the kernels we extract; Pople (1958), already in our corpus, is the ring-current source they cite. `de-gortari-2010-time-averaging-nmr-chemical-shifts-mlf-peptide` and `karp-2014-md-shifts-disordered-proteins` are the conceptual complement: averaging shifts over MD ensembles versus restraining MD with shifts. `bratholm-2017-procs15-qm-chemical-shielding-refinement` plays a similar refinement role with QM-derived shifts. JACS 131 (2009) 13894-95 (CamShift origin), Vallurupalli, Hansen, Kay PNAS 105 (2008) 11766-71 (excited-state RDC structure of Abp1p SH3 — the ground-truth dataset for the SH3 example here), Cavalli et al. PNAS 104 (2007) 9615-20 (Cheshire), Shen et al. PNAS 105 (2008) 4685-90 (CS-Rosetta), Robustelli et al. JPCB 113 (2009) 7890-96 (the MC predecessor of this paper).

Implications for our work. CS-MD treats shifts as constraints that pull conformations toward consistency with experiment; we treat conformations as input and shifts (or their kernel surrogates) as the dependent quantity, asking how much of the per-atom shift signal a fixed kernel set captures across an ensemble. The two stances are not in competition — they share the same physics — but they place the kernels at opposite ends of the inference. The paper's loop-region failure is a methodological warning relevant beyond CS-MD: a shift predictor parameterized on crystallographic conformations can be systematically off where solution behaviour diverges, and the effect bleeds into anything using CamShift-style predictions as a fitness function. Per-frame extraction across MD frames is the natural place to characterize that drift quantitatively rather than fold it into an effective tolerance. The differentiability point is also load-bearing: smoothness of the shift predictor in atomic coordinates is what made MD rather than MC the productive sampler here, and our kernel extractor has that smoothness by construction — relevant context if a future reader asks whether our kernels could in principle replace CamShift in a CS-MD scheme.
