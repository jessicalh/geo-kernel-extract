This Perspective and research study examines how protein conformational dynamics influence 15N and 13C chemical shifts in dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) bound to trimethoprim. The authors compare automated fragmentation quantum mechanics/molecular mechanics (AF-QM/MM) predictions against room-temperature solution NMR and 105 K dynamic nuclear polarization-enhanced solid-state NMR (DNP-SSNMR) data. The central argument is that conformational averaging over molecular dynamics (MD) trajectories substantially improves the correlation between predicted and experimental isotropic shifts, while the breadth of predicted shift distributions captures the inhomogeneous line broadening observed in cryogenic spectra.

The authors performed a 1000 ns explicit-solvent MD simulation starting from a 2.5 Å X-ray structure of DHFR:TMP. From this trajectory, 20 snapshots spaced 50 ns apart were selected, energy-minimized with heavy-atom RMSD constraints (<0.3 Å), and processed with AF-QM/MM to compute 15N, 13Cα, and 13C′ isotropic shifts. A separate 100 ps segment sampled every 100 fs was analyzed for torsion angle dynamics. Experimental comparisons used solution-state NMR data and DNP-SSNMR spectra of selectively 13C,15N-labeled DHFR at 105 K. Correlation coefficients and root-mean-square errors were calculated to quantify prediction accuracy against single-conformer X-ray structures versus ensemble-averaged MD snapshots.

Averaging QM/MM predictions over the 20-snapshot ensemble improved correlation with experimental solution shifts to r = 0.73 for 15N and r = 0.94 for 13Cα, reducing RMSE to 4.8 ppm and 1.7 ppm, respectively, compared to 7.9 ppm and 3.6 ppm for the single X-ray conformer. 13C′ predictions showed weaker agreement (r = 0.51, RMSE = 2.3 ppm). Predicted 15N shifts varied by up to 25 ppm across the trajectory. For Ile60-Ile61, the backbone torsion angles ψi and φi+1 fluctuated by ~60° on picosecond timescales, anticorrelated (r = −0.89), producing a crankshaft-like rocking of the amide plane. Ile61 15N isotropic shifts varied by up to 5 ppm over a 5° range of I60ψ. The distribution of predicted shifts matched the contour widths of 105 K SSNMR spectra. Hydrogen-bond angle θ correlated strongly with ψ, whereas distance showed weak correlation with shifts. State-switching residence times were 0.89 ± 0.18 ps, 0.34 ± 0.05 ps, and 0.23 ± 0.02 ps.

The work builds on prior QM/MM benchmarking by He and Merz (2009) and Swails et al. (2015), and extends conformational averaging studies by Exner et al. (2012) and Dracínský et al. (2013). It engages with empirical shift predictors like SHIFTX2 (Han et al., 2011) and SPARTA+ (Shen & Bax, 2010), noting their limitation to thermally averaged basins. The torsion-angle dependence aligns with Le and Oldfield (1994) and Wang and Jardetzky (2004), while the cryogenic line-broadening interpretation connects to Su and Hong (2011), Gupta et al. (2019), and Yi (2023). The authors also reference Polenova’s group (Kraus et al., 2020) for QM/MM tensor studies and Case (2020) for quantum chemical shift estimation in biomolecules.

The paper supports the thesis requirement that geometric kernels must capture ensemble-averaged structural signals rather than static coordinates. The correlation improvements (r = 0.73 for 15N and r = 0.94 for 13Cα when averaging is applied) indicate that kernel calibration should incorporate torsion-angle distributions and hydrogen-bond angular variations. The finding that ψ fluctuations drive 15N shift variations up to 25 ppm suggests that backbone torsion descriptors require sufficient effective dimensionality to represent conformational breadth. Because the study reports only isotropic shifts and does not compute or calibrate rank-2 shielding tensors, direct implications for tensor preservation are limited; however, the ensemble-sampling protocol and restricted minimization procedure provide a practical template for generating training distributions that preserve structural heterogeneity during kernel calibration.
