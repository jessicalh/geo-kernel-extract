Robustelli, P., Kohlhoff, K., Cavalli, A., & Vendruscolo, M. (2010). Using NMR Chemical Shifts as Structural Restraints in Molecular Dynamics Simulations of Proteins. Structure, 18, 923–933. DOI: 10.1016/j.str.2010.04.016. 11 pages. No funding or credit notes are stated.

This is a research paper presenting a methodological procedure for incorporating NMR backbone chemical shifts as differentiable structural restraints in molecular dynamics simulations. The authors argue that expressing chemical shifts as continuous, differentiable functions of atomic coordinates enables efficient conformational sampling via molecular dynamics, overcoming limitations of previous Monte Carlo approaches that relied on discontinuous prediction methods. The study targets computational structural biology and NMR spectroscopy, demonstrating the method on 12 globular proteins and a transiently populated excited state.

The authors implement the CamShift algorithm, which predicts 1Hα, 13Cα, 13Cβ, 13C', 1H, and 15N chemical shifts using polynomial functions of interatomic distances, dihedral angles, ring currents, and through-space interactions. A soft-square harmonic penalty function converts deviations between calculated and experimental shifts into forces. The penalty features a flat bottom of width nσ_j (n=1), a harmonic region up to a cutoff x0 (4.0 ppm for 1Hα/1H, 20.0 ppm for 13Cα/13Cβ/13C'/15N), and a hyperbolic tangent tail (γ=20) to maintain continuous derivatives. Forces are computed via the gradient of the penalty with respect to Cartesian coordinates. Replica exchange simulations use the AMBER03 force field, generalized Born implicit solvation, SHAKE constraints on all bond lengths and angles, and 21 temperature replicas (270–515 K). The penalty weight α was set to 1 during simulation to prevent force-induced instabilities, with energies recalculated post-simulation using α=5. Starting conformations are partially unfolded structures with backbone rmsd 3.5–7.0 Å from reference structures.

In 11 of 12 test cases, the simulations identified near-native structures as the lowest energy conformation (recalculated with α=5), with backbone rmsd values ranging from 0.84 to 2.15 Å. Control simulations without chemical shift restraints successfully folded only one protein. The average backbone rmsd of the lowest-sampled structures was 1.24 Å, with the most accurate sampled structure reaching 0.62 Å. For protein NE1242 (PDB 2jv8), the method failed due to a disordered loop region; removing restraints for residues 28–58 and 68–73 reduced the lowest-energy rmsd to 2.48 Å and the lowest-sampled rmsd to 1.37 Å. Folding from a fully extended conformation for GB3 (2oed) failed on nanosecond timescales, trapping the system in a local minimum at 10.82 Å rmsd. When combined with relaxation dispersion-derived RDCs for the peptide-bound excited state of the Abp1p SH3 domain, the five lowest-energy structures achieved a backbone rmsd of 0.51 Å and RDC Q-factors of 0.31–0.34 for Pf1 phage and 0.29–0.35 for PEG/hexanol alignment media.

The work builds directly on the CamShift prediction framework (Kohlhoff et al., 2009) and extends the authors’ prior Monte Carlo implementation (Robustelli et al., 2009). It positions the method alongside molecular fragment replacement approaches such as Cheshire (Cavalli et al., 2007), CS-Rosetta (Das et al., 2009; Shen et al., 2008, 2009), and CS23D (Wishart et al., 2008). The authors reference standard NMR restraint integration protocols (Schwieters et al., 2006; Brunger, 2007), relaxation dispersion techniques for excited states (Vallurupalli et al., 2008; Baldwin & Kay, 2009), and foundational chemical shift models (Wishart & Case, 2001; Neal et al., 2003; Shen & Bax, 2007). Random coil shift corrections and loop flexibility are contextualized using De Simone et al. (2009), Schwarzinger et al. (2001), and Berjanskii & Wishart (2007).

The paper demonstrates that a predictive model for scalar NMR observables can be converted into a differentiable penalty function that generates forces for MD integration. This establishes a methodological parallel to the thesis pipeline, where geometric kernels are calibrated against DFT shielding values to produce a functional mapping. However, the CamShift model relies on empirical distance polynomials rather than first-principles tensor geometry, and it operates on isotropic chemical shifts rather than rank-2 shielding tensors. The study does not address tensor preservation, DFT calibration, or per-element complexity. Its primary relevance lies in validating the feasibility of embedding NMR-derived restraints into MD force fields and combining multiple observable types within a single simulation framework. The failure to fold from extended conformations on nanosecond timescales suggests that local structural information in shifts captures the signal for conformational guidance but does not replace global topological constraints, which aligns with the thesis assumption that local kernels require global assembly.
