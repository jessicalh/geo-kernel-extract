Facelli, J.C. (2011) "Chemical shift tensors: Theory and application to molecular structural problems." Progress in Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy 58(3-4), 176–201. DOI: 10.1016/j.pnmrs.2010.10.003. NIH Public Access (PMC 2012 May 01). Single author, 26 pages, approximately 231 references. Department of Biomedical Informatics, Center for High Performance Computing, University of Utah.

A pedagogical review of chemical shift tensor theory, computation, and applications, aimed at practitioners entering the field from either NMR or computational chemistry backgrounds. The paper covers the quantum-mechanical derivation of the shielding tensor from the electronic Hamiltonian, practical methods for gauge-origin handling in finite basis calculations, DFT-based implementations, intermolecular and solid-state effects on shielding, available software, expected accuracy of predictions, and a survey of structural applications including the emerging field of NMR crystallography.

Theory and methods. The shielding tensor σ_αβ = ∂²E/∂μ_α∂B_β is derived from second-order Rayleigh-Schrödinger perturbation theory and partitioned into diamagnetic and paramagnetic contributions whose individual values depend on gauge origin but whose sum is invariant for exact wave functions. For finite basis sets, gauge-invariance strategies include GIAO (Ditchfield; Cheeseman-Pulay implementation), IGLO (Kutzelnigg), LORG, IGAIM (Bader's topological-atom approach), and CSGT. Facelli cites Gauss & Stanton's 2008 review as the most comprehensive modern treatment. Key methodological observation: "most of the modern exchange-correlation functionals give shieldings of similar quality… shieldings are much less sensitive to the selection of the exchange-correlation functional than other molecular properties."

Accuracy and applications. Expected relative accuracies for DFT shielding calculations: ¹H 2–3%, ¹³C 1.4–1.9%, ¹⁵N ~10%, ¹⁷O ~14%. Further methodological point worth emphasising: "fortuitous cancellation of errors in individual tensor components can lead to artificially high agreement in the isotropic chemical shifts" — so tensor-component comparisons (e.g. via the Alderman et al. icosahedral representation) are more rigorous than isotropic-shift agreement. Applications surveyed include organic stereochemistry (combined cRMSD = RMSD(¹³C) + 10 × RMSD(¹H) as discriminator), biological molecules via the Vila-Arnautova-Scheraga fragment approach (Ac-G-X-G-NMe capping for protein ¹³Cα prediction, tested on 48-residue PDB 2JVD), solid-state polymers, coals, and NMR crystallography of materials like paclitaxel polymorphs (Grant group) and bacterial cellulose Iα.

Implications for our work, widely considered. This is the thesis methods-chapter anchor for shielding calculations. Four direct cite-points: (i) the "fortuitous cancellation" quote supports the T2-is-sacred argument — isotropic agreement can hide tensor disagreement, so our per-component T2 analysis is the more rigorous test; (ii) Facelli's observation that shieldings are relatively insensitive to functional choice among modern functionals supports our r²SCAN adoption without needing a deep functional-choice defense; (iii) Buckingham's solvent classification (σ = σ_0 + σ_b + σ_a + σ_w + σ_E, attributed here to Buckingham 1960) justifies why explicit-solvent MD rather than an Onsager-style continuum reaction field is required for polar protein environments; (iv) the Vila-Arnautova-Scheraga fragment approach mirrors our cluster-DFT strategy for the 260-DFT calibration on 10 proteins — a cite-able precedent. The expected-accuracy numbers (¹H 2–3%, ¹³C 1.4–1.9%) give order-of-magnitude anchors for our calibration residuals and a floor against which to measure whether our kernel decompositions recover tensor-level accuracy beyond the isotropic-shift ceiling.
