Venetos, M. C.; Wen, M.; Persson, K. A. Machine Learning Full NMR Chemical Shift Tensors of Silicon Oxides with Equivariant Graph Neural Networks. J. Phys. Chem. A 2023, 127, 2388−2398. DOI: 10.1021/acs.jpca.2c07530. 11 pages, 73 references. Supported by NSF Grant DIBBS OAC 1640899, a Presidential Frontier Faculty Program at the University of Houston, and the U.S. Department of Energy, Office of Basic Energy Sciences, Materials Sciences and Engineering Division under Contract DE-AC02-05-CH11231.

This research paper evaluates machine learning approaches for predicting full 29Si NMR chemical shift tensors in silicate materials, contrasting rotation-invariant and rotation-equivariant graph neural networks. The authors argue that treating tensor parameters as independent scalars discards structural symmetry information, necessitating equivariant architectures to preserve the rank-2 tensor nature of the NMR measurement. The work targets computational materials scientists and NMR crystallographers seeking rapid, high-fidelity tensor predictions to accelerate structural refinement.

The study uses a dataset of 1387 unique silicon sites across 421 relaxed silicate structures, sourced from ab initio VASP calculations reported by Sun et al. Data were split 8:1:1 at the structure level to prevent leakage. The authors benchmarked linear ridge regression with SOAP descriptors, the DimeNet++ invariant GNN, and a Tensor Field Network equivariant GNN implemented via the MatTEN package. Training targets included multiple tensor conventions and irreducible representations. The optimal configuration employed symmetric irreducible representations as the target with an l1 norm loss function.

The equivariant GNN predicts the full Cartesian chemical shift tensor to a mean absolute error of 1.05 ppm, capturing the signal from the ab initio reference values across magnitude, anisotropy, and orientation. When diagonalized, the model yields an eigenvalue MAE of 3.05 ppm, a 53% improvement over the best invariant model (6.44 ppm). Isotropic shift prediction achieves an MAE of 2.82 ppm, outperforming the LRR-SOAP benchmark (5.87 ppm) and historic analytical models (3.60 ppm for Q4 sites, 8.77 ppm for Q3 sites). Per-Qn analysis shows the lowest isotropic error for high-symmetry Td sites (Q4: 1.52 ppm; Q0: 1.46 ppm) and higher errors for underrepresented sites (Q1: 8.58 ppm; Q2: 6.95 ppm). Tensor orientation errors increase for highly spherical tensors and poorly sampled C2/C3 environments, though shape parameters remain well-correlated.

The work builds on prior ML applications to NMR, specifically the LRR-SOAP model for isotropic shifts by Chaker et al., and analytical correlations by Engelhardt and Radeglia and Grimmer et al. It extends geometric deep learning frameworks by Cohen and Welling and Satorras et al. to tensorial NMR targets, drawing on tensor field network theory by Thomas et al. and irreducible representation formalisms by Grandinetti et al. The dataset originates from Sun et al., and benchmarking references historic NMR crystallography protocols by Brouwer et al. and Pickard et al.

For this project, the paper demonstrates that enforcing equivariance during training preserves rank-2 tensor structure more effectively than predicting decoupled scalar parameters, aligning with the thesis requirement for T2 preservation. The reported MAE benchmarks provide a performance ceiling for ML-based tensor prediction on crystalline networks. The finding that shape parameters correlate reliably even when orientation or isotropic values degrade suggests that kernel calibration may prioritize tensor geometry over absolute shift values. The study does not address protein systems or ring-current kernels, but its treatment of irreducible representations and loss function selection for tensor targets offers a methodological reference for calibrating geometric kernels to DFT shielding values.
