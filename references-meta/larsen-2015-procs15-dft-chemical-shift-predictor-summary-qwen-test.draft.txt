Larsen, A. S., Bratholm, L. A., Christensen, A. S., Channir, M., & Jensen, J. H. (2015). ProCS15: a DFT-based chemical shift predictor for backbone and Cβ atoms in proteins. *PeerJ*, 3, e1344. 19 pages. References: 70. Funding: Lundbeck Foundation and Danish e-Infrastructure Cooporation. Acknowledgements: Osvaldo Martin, Jorge Vila, Xiao He.

This is a research paper introducing ProCS15, a computational program that predicts isotropic chemical shielding values for backbone and Cβ atoms in proteins from a given structure in under one second. The authors position the method as a quantum mechanical alternative to empirical shift predictors, arguing that DFT-based approaches retain greater sensitivity to local protein geometry, which may make them more suitable for structure refinement. The study benchmarks the predictor against full DFT calculations and experimental NMR shifts for Ubiquitin and GB3, while also evaluating performance across single X-ray structures and NMR-derived conformational ensembles. The target audience includes computational chemists and structural biologists working with NMR data, and the paper functions as both a methodological description and a comparative accuracy study.

ProCS15 decomposes the isotropic shielding σ_i into additive contributions: backbone dihedral effects (σ_BB^i), neighboring side-chain effects (Δσ_BB^{i±1}), hydrogen-bonding to the amide group and Hα (Δσ_HB^i, Δσ_HαB^i), ring-current effects (Δσ_RC^i), and explicit water corrections (Δσ_w^i). The backbone and side-chain terms are derived from approximately 2.35 million OPBE/6-31G(d,p)//PM6 GIAO calculations on capped tripeptides (Ac-AXA-NMe) scanned over φ, ψ, and χ angles on a 20° grid, with CPCM solvation (ε=78). Hydrogen-bond terms are parameterized using N-methyl acetamide and Ac-A-NMe models scanned over distance, angle, and dihedral coordinates. Ring current is modeled as a point dipole with an 8 Å cutoff. Missing conformations are filled via interpolation. Predicted shieldings are converted to shifts via δ_i = b - aσ_i, where a and b are fitted per atom type against experimental data.

ProCS15 shielding values correlate with full OPBE/6-31G(d,p)//PM6-D3H+ calculations, capturing the structural signal with r² values ranging from 0.25 to 0.71 (r = 0.50–0.84), while RMSD values remain between 1.6 and 2.5 ppm for carbon, 0.6 and 0.8 ppm for hydrogen, and 4.4 and 4.5 ppm for nitrogen. Against experimental shifts, ProCS15 achieves RMSD values of 1.6–2.0 ppm for carbon, 0.3–0.6 ppm for hydrogen, and 4.0–4.3 ppm for nitrogen. Empirical predictors (CamShift, ShiftX2, Sparta+, PPM One, shAIC) achieve lower RMSD (0.5–1.1 ppm for C, 0.1–0.4 ppm for H, 1.8–3.0 ppm for N) and higher correlation. Using NMR-derived ensembles for Ubiquitin reduces ProCS15 RMSD by 0.1–0.7 ppm across atom types, whereas empirical methods show minimal or inconsistent improvement. Two outliers in GB3 (Ala20 Cβ and Gln2 HN) are traced to short-range interactions absent from the parameterization grids.

The work extends a prior amide-proton-only predictor (Christensen et al., 2013) and adopts ring-current parameters from Christensen, Sauer & Jensen (2011). It engages with systematic DFT benchmarking studies by Vila, Baldoni & Scheraga (2009) and Vila, Arnautova & Scheraga (2010) on functional and basis-set performance, and with fragmentation-based DFT approaches by Zhu, He & Zhang (2012) and Zhu, Zhang & He (2013). The sensitivity argument draws on Parker, Houk & Jensen (2006) and Sumowski et al. (2014), which contrast QM and empirical methods. Direct comparisons are made to CheShift-2 (Martin et al., 2013) and empirical predictors including CamShift (Kohlhoff et al., 2009), Sparta+ (Shen & Bax, 2010), ShiftX2 (Han et al., 2011), PPM One (Li & Brüschweiler, 2015), and shAIC (Nielsen, Eghbalnia & Nielsen, 2012).

ProCS15 provides a quantitative demonstration that DFT-derived shielding predictions capture structural sensitivity more faithfully than empirical methods, reinforcing the thesis premise that geometric kernels must preserve fine conformational detail. However, the method operates entirely on isotropic shielding scalars and relies on discrete lookup tables rather than continuous geometric kernels. Its additive decomposition of shielding terms does not map onto the rank-2 tensor preservation framework developed in the thesis. The linear calibration (δ = b - aσ) parallels the thesis calibration step, but the underlying signal extraction is fundamentally different. The paper is best used as a benchmark for structural sensitivity and as a reference for DFT parameterization choices (OPBE/6-31G(d,p), CPCM solvation, tripeptide fragmentation), rather than as a direct template for kernel construction or tensor decomposition.
