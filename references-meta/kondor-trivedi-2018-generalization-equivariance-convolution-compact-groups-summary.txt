Kondor, R. & Trivedi, S. (2018) "On the Generalization of Equivariance and Convolution in Neural Networks to the Action of Compact Groups." Proceedings of the 35th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), Stockholm, PMLR 80. 9 pages (body) + 5 pages supplementary (see companion `*-SI.pdf`). Departments of Statistics and Computer Science, University of Chicago (Kondor); Toyota Technological Institute at Chicago (Trivedi).

A theoretical paper establishing the rigorous mathematical basis for equivariant neural networks under the action of compact groups. The paper does not present new algorithms; it provides the language and theorems that later architectures (Tensor Field Networks, e3nn, NequIP, MACE) implement. It is the backing theory for Kondor's own 2025 PNAS pedagogical review, which is in our corpus.

Central result (Theorem 1): a feed-forward neural network is equivariant to the action of a compact group G on its inputs **if and only if** each layer of the network implements a generalised form of convolution derived from the group-theoretic convolution formula

    (f * g)(u) = ∫_G f(u·v⁻¹) g(v) dμ(v)

where integration is against the Haar measure μ. The "if" direction was implicit in earlier work (group-equivariant CNNs of Cohen & Welling 2016 and others); the "only if" direction — that convolutional structure is **necessary**, not merely sufficient, for equivariance — is the paper's novel contribution.

Method / machinery. The proof requires careful handling of the fact that network activations live in a sequence of homogeneous spaces X_0, X_1, …, X_L (quotient spaces of G), not functions on G itself. The authors develop the required generalised notion of group convolution across quotient spaces. Definitions and notation occupy Sections 2-3 (equivariance, transitivity, homogeneous spaces, commutative diagrams); Section 4 develops convolution on groups and quotient spaces; Section 5 proves Theorem 1 in the general case; Section 6 works the theorem through concrete examples (translations, rotations, permutations, graphs) to show how various practical networks fit the framework.

Relations to our corpus. Implications for our work. Direct theoretical backing for the claim in our Stage 3 design space that any SO(3)-equivariant tensor-output model must, necessarily, implement the integral-convolution structure described by this theorem — not merely that such a structure is one way to get equivariance. The "only if" direction is the strong claim: if the thesis defence reaches "why must a Stage 3 equivariant GNN take this specific form?", Kondor-Trivedi 2018 is the citation. For most writing purposes the 2025 PNAS review is the pedagogical cite; this 2018 paper is the rigorous backing when rigor is demanded.
