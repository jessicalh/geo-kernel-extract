Klukowski, P., Riek, R. & Güntert, P. (2022). Rapid protein assignments and structures from raw NMR spectra with the deep learning technique ARTINA. Nature Communications, 13, 6151. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-022-33879-5. 12 pages, 46 references. Funding: EU Horizon 2020 Marie Skłodowska-Curie grant agreement No 891690 (P.K.); JSPS Grant-in-Aid for Scientific Research 20 K06508 (P.G.). Acknowledgements note contributions from Drs. Frédéric Allain, Fred Damberger, Hideo Iwai, Harindranath Kadavath, Julien Orts, and Dean Strotz for unpublished spectra.

This is a research paper presenting ARTINA, a fully automated machine learning pipeline for NMR resonance assignment and protein structure determination. The authors aim to replace the traditionally manual, expert-dependent NMR analysis workflow with a system that requires only raw multidimensional spectra and the protein sequence as input. The method is evaluated on a benchmark of 100 proteins (1329 spectra, 35–175 residues) and is positioned as a tool for non-experts, reducing structure determination time from weeks to hours.

ARTINA integrates several machine learning models with established NMR algorithms. Visual spectrum analysis uses a peak-picking ResNet (pp-ResNet) and a deconvolution ResNet (deconv-ResNet) to identify and resolve overlapping cross-peaks. A kernel density estimator (KDE) handles signal unfolding in folded spectra. Chemical shift assignment and refinement are performed by FLYA, guided by a deep graph neural network (GNN) trained on BMRB/PDB records to predict missing shifts. Structure calculation employs CYANA with torsion angle restraints from TALOS-N. Ten candidate structures are generated with varying NOESY peak confidence thresholds and ranked by a gradient boosted tree (GBT) model using 96 structural features. The pipeline executes three interchangeable refinement cycles.

In a 5-fold cross-validation on the benchmark, ARTINA reproduces reference structures with a median backbone RMSD of 1.44 Å. Overall chemical shift assignment accuracy is 90.39% (96.03% backbone, 86.50% side-chain), with core residues generally assigned more accurately. The system identifies an average of 2198 distance restraints per protein, which correlates only weakly with backbone RMSD (linear correlation coefficient −0.38). A predicted RMSD metric correlates with actual RMSD at a linear correlation coefficient of 0.77, with a mean deviation of 0.35 Å. Three refinement cycles reduce the median backbone RMSD from 1.56 Å to 1.44 Å. The inclusion of 4D CC-NOESY spectra improves median RMSD by 0.15 Å but has less than 1% impact on assignment accuracy. Structural deviations are primarily localized to terminal secondary structure elements and flexible loops.

The work builds on established NMR software (FLYA, CYANA, TALOS-N) and previous deep learning applications for NMR (NMRNet, DEEP picker, AUTOPSY, CYPICK). It references the CASD-NMR competition and the BMRB database as foundational data sources. The authors position ARTINA alongside recent advances in structure prediction, notably AlphaFold, and suggest future integration of AlphaFold models with reduced NMR datasets. The bibliography also cites foundational methods for automated NOE assignment, structure validation (RPF, ANSSUR), and consensus bundle calculation.

This paper addresses end-to-end automated structure determination from raw NMR spectra using deep learning and CYANA-based torsion angle dynamics. It does not engage with NMR shielding tensors, DFT calibration, or geometric kernel modeling. The outputs are backbone coordinates and chemical shift assignments, not shielding values or rank-2 tensor fields. While the benchmark structures could theoretically serve as input geometries for applying geometric kernels, the paper itself provides no methodology for shielding prediction, tensor preservation, or calibration against quantum chemical calculations. Its relevance to the thesis is strictly as a source of automated protein structures from NMR data, with no direct methodological overlap.
