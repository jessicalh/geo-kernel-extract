A 2026 review of AI/ML for NMR chemical shift prediction by Piroozi (Nazarbayev), Thomas (NYU Abu Dhabi), and Kammakakam (Nazarbayev, corresponding). The audience is the chemistry-and-spectroscopy ML practitioner. The stance is positive: the field is described as transitioning from descriptor-based methods and feedforward networks to graph neural networks (GNNs) and transformer architectures that learn molecular representations directly from 2D and 3D structures, achieving DFT-comparable accuracy at orders-of-magnitude lower cost. The review covers methods (tree-based, kernel, FFNN, GNN, transformer, hybrid QM/ML), datasets, performance metrics, applications, and AI beyond shifts (spectral reconstruction, denoising, peak picking, virtual decoupling), and closes with four open challenges.

Table 1 catalogues 29 ML methods. State-of-the-art systems typically report 1H MAE 0.07–0.30 ppm and 13C MAE 0.7–3 ppm against DFT or experimental references. Headlines worth naming: ShiftML / ShiftML2 (kernel ridge regression with SOAP descriptors, GIPAW-DFT-trained, R² 0.97–0.99 in molecular solids); UCBShift (random forest with sequence and structural alignment for proteins, beats SHIFTX2 and SPARTA+); IMPRESSION (KRR with FCHL kernel, millisecond predictions, variance filter as on-the-fly UQ); NMRNet (SE(3) transformer, pretrained on 4.8M+ structures across liquids and solids); IMPRESSION-G2 (transformer GNN, simultaneous shifts + scalar couplings for 1H/13C/15N/19F in <50 ms); DU8ML (ML correction layer on B3LYP/6-31G(d), used to revise ~20% of literature alkaloid assignments); PROSPRE (GNN trained on DFT8K then fine-tuned, MAE 0.10 ppm on 600,000 NP-MRD molecules). All headline accuracies are reported on isotropic shifts; MAE/RMSE dominate, with R² in a minority. Speedups over DFT range 100× to 10⁶×.

Three threads run through the review. First, dataset scarcity drives architecture: synthetic-data pretraining (NMRNet, ShiftML2 with deliberate thermal perturbation), transfer learning (PROSPRE), and weakly-supervised loss functions (Kang et al.) are explicit responses to limited experimental shifts. Second, equivariant 3D models are converging as the dominant pattern — SE(3) (NMRNet), MACE-OFF23, PAiNN, DimeNet/SchNet/SphereNet. Third, uncertainty quantification is identified as a remaining gap and the named exemplars (iShiftML's ensemble error, IMPRESSION's variance filter, Bhadauria's variational Gaussian-process regression) are still rare. The challenges section flags four open problems: generalisability beyond training distribution, data quality and standardisation, UQ, and workflow integration. The "beyond chemical shifts" subsection names J-couplings, T1/T2 relaxation times, and diffusion as the next observables — and never names shielding tensors (chemical shift anisotropy, the rank-2 quantity) as a prediction target.

For our corpus, the review's positioning matters more than its content. The 96-reference bibliography contains exactly two tensor-aware entries: Wylie et al. PNAS 108 (2011) 16974–16979 ("Ultrahigh resolution protein structures using NMR chemical shift tensors") and Kellner et al. J. Phys. Chem. Lett. 16 (2025) 8714–8722 ("A deep learning model for chemical shieldings in molecular organic solids including anisotropy"). Wylie 2011 is cited only in passing on shift sensitivity; Kellner 2025 is cited once in a UQ context and is not given its own table row. Otherwise the entire 26-row methods table reports isotropic shifts, even where the underlying training data are tensor-shaped (iShiftML trains on atomic chemical shielding tensors that are then converted to scalar shifts). SHIFTX2 (Han et al. 2011) is already in our corpus as `han-2011-shiftx2-protein-chemical-shift-prediction`. SPARTA+ is named as a benchmark but not on disk. Robustelli/Vendruscolo's CamShift line is not cited at all — protein-NMR coverage in this review runs through SHIFTX2, UCBShift, and Yang's 2021 GNN. Acquisition pointers, none on disk: Wylie 2011 (most thesis-aligned in the entire bibliography), Kellner 2025 (the recent tensor-prediction outlier), Paruzzo / ShiftML 2018 Nat Commun (the R²-as-metric exemplar), Cordova / ShiftML2 2022, Gerrard / IMPRESSION 2020 (variance-filter UQ), Li / iShiftML 2024 (ANI-1 + ensemble UQ), Yiu / IMPRESSION-G2 2025 (multi-parameter), Bhadauria / CASCADE-2.0 2025 chemrxiv (heavy elements + variational GPR), Sajed / PROSPRE 2024.

Implications for our work. The review's argumentative arc — "the field is moving from manually-designed NN to end-to-end DL that learns chemical realism from data" — describes a trajectory the thesis is deliberately not on. We hold to designed kernels with explicit physics; the review treats designed-feature methods (HOSE codes, descriptor-based NNs, classical regression) as an obsolete generation. Two reframings the thesis can lean on. First, "chemical realism" is identified as an open challenge that more training data alone has not solved; our stance that physics commitments enter via kernel choice rather than training-data composition is a different answer to the same question, and the review's silence on geometric-kernel methods reads as a gap rather than a refutation. Second, the "beyond chemical shifts" subsection's enumeration of J/T1/T2-relaxation/diffusion as the next-target observables — with shielding tensors absent — is direct evidence that even a 2026 comprehensive review treats T2 (rank-2) prediction as a footnote rather than a frontier. The two tensor-aware bibliography entries (Wylie 2011 in proteins, Kellner 2025 in solids) bracket the methodological territory the thesis claims. Several methods in the table (UCBShift's feature catalogue including S² order parameters and ring currents, Yang 2021's differentiable-by-construction GNN, IMPRESSION's variance filter, Bhadauria's variational GPR for UQ) are individual best-of-breed comparators for specific Stage-3 design questions if we ever extend beyond ridge regression. The review is most valuable as a single-source roundup of the contemporary ML-NMR landscape; the bibliography is the deliverable, not the synthesis.
