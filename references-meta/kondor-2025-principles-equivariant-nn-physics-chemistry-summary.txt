Kondor, R. (2025) "The principles behind equivariant neural networks for physics and chemistry." PNAS 122(41), e2415656122. DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2415656122. Open access via PMC12541325. Single-author Perspective, 10 pages, 66 references.

Declared competing interest: Kondor is inventor on US patent 11,934,478 (subject adjacent to this article) and founder of Intertwiner Inc., which develops equivariant-NN technologies. The mathematical content stands independently; architectural prescriptions warrant being read alongside independent voices in the field.

A pedagogical review of equivariant neural networks — networks whose internal representations transform predictably when the input is rotated, translated, or has its identical particles permuted. Kondor's stated goal is to explain why equivariant architectures look the way they do, rather than recapitulate the historical development. Scope is restricted to point-cloud data (molecules, particles in Euclidean space) and compact groups; non-compact groups like the Lorentz group, and data on meshes or voxels, are flagged as beyond scope.

Three mathematical results carry the paper. (i) Equivariance versus invariance: an invariant network gives the same output when the input is rotated; an equivariant network allows the output to transform in a coordinated way, which is what you need when the prediction itself is a vector or tensor. (ii) The Peter–Weyl theorem: any representation of a compact group decomposes into irreducible representations, or "irreps"; for SO(3) these are indexed by ℓ = 0, 1, 2, ... and realized concretely as spherical harmonics and Wigner D-matrices. (iii) Equivariant linear layers reduce, via Theorem 1 of the paper and Schur's Lemma, to a sequence of matrix multiplications — one matrix per irrep channel. For nonlinearities, Kondor argues the Clebsch–Gordan (CG) product — tensor product of two irrep vectors, decomposed back into irreps — is the natural polynomial equivariant operation, matching the polynomial or rational form of most physical laws.

The intellectual narrative threads Cohen & Welling 2016 group-equivariant CNNs as the modern origin, with antecedents in Wood & Shawe-Taylor 1996 and foundations in Weyl's 1946 The Classical Groups. The landscape is laid out in three branches: invariant descriptors (SOAP of Bartók-Kondor-Csányi 2013, ACE of Drautz 2019, Coulomb matrix, Behler–Parrinello symmetry functions); equivariant architectures for 3D point clouds (Tensor Field Networks of Thomas-Smidt 2018, NequIP of Batzner 2022, MACE of Batatia, Allegro of Musaelian); and permutation-equivariant graph networks (Deep Sets, message-passing GNNs, Kondor's P-tensors). Libraries called out: E3NN (Geiger & Smidt 2022) and GElib.

Papers the review positions as worth having but absent from our references: Cohen & Welling 2016 (group CNN origin), Drautz 2019 (ACE), Batatia et al. (MACE). Papers already flagged [CORE] in our annotated bibliography for acquisition: Thomas-Smidt 2018, Geiger-Smidt 2022, Batzner 2022, Bartók-Kondor-Csányi 2013. A direct quote worth marking: invariant-only descriptors "factor out the action of the symmetry group right at the outset, [missing] the opportunity to take into account group-action relative information about the interaction between parts of the system in higher layers."

Implications for our work. The T0/T1/T2 decomposition of our shielding tensors is exactly the SO(3) irrep structure this review formalises, so the "T2 is sacred" rule is structural, not stylistic — the methods-chapter citation for why we track T0, T1, and T2 separately. For Stage 3 or any future learned-model work, the design-space reference: any equivariant tensor-output GNN we build must implement Theorem 1's per-irrep matrix operations plus CG nonlinearities. For committee questions of the "why not just train a GNN?" form, the review makes concrete what such a network would be constrained to do — and by implication, where hand-crafted physics kernels retain interpretive and compute advantages.
