This review article surveys structurally oriented experimental datasets from nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and room-temperature (RT) protein crystallography for benchmarking protein force fields. Targeted at computational researchers developing or validating molecular dynamics simulations and machine learning ensembles, it argues that robust evaluation requires testing against peptides, folded proteins, and intrinsically disordered proteins. The authors categorize observables by experimental uncertainty, simulation convergence speed, structural scope (local versus tertiary), and consensus on calculation best practices, recommending a tiered benchmarking strategy that prioritizes low-uncertainty, fast-converging metrics.

The paper presents no new simulations or quantum calculations. It systematically reviews methodologies for computing NMR and crystallographic observables from trajectories. For NMR, it details empirical chemical shift predictors (SHIFTS, PROSHIFT, Camshift, SPARTA+, SHIFTX2, PPM, UCB-Shift, GraphNMR), the Karplus equation for scalar J-couplings, alignment tensor fitting for residual dipolar couplings (RDCs), effective distance models for nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE) spectroscopy, the Lipari-Szabo model-free approach for spin relaxation, and the Solomon-Bloembergen equation for paramagnetic relaxation enhancement (PRE). For crystallography, it covers Bragg diffraction intensity calculation, Debye-Waller (B-factor) computation, alternate conformation occupancy analysis, and diffuse scattering modeling. It outlines setup protocols for solvation, ion placement, and thermodynamic ensembles, and analysis protocols for equilibration, convergence, and statistical significance.

The review catalogs specific datasets with quantitative details. The Beauchamp dataset contains 524 chemical shifts and J-couplings for 31 short peptides and ubiquitin. The Robustelli dataset spans 21 proteins/peptides with over 9,000 data points. The Stroet dataset includes 52 structures with 13 NMR-derived models. The Mao dataset comprises 41 folded proteins. Spin relaxation data cover approximately 350 proteins in the BMRB, with a curated set of 133 used for the Dyna-1 model. Crystallographic datasets range from 0.65 Å to 1.74 Å resolution, including HEWL (PDB IDs 2LZT, 6O2H, 4LZT, 2VB1), Crambin (3U7T, 9EWK, R-factor 0.0591), CypA (4YUO), and PTP1B (6B8X). Regarding predictor performance, the SPARTA+ standard deviation is 0.94 ppm on its training database; C′ chemical shift predictions for disordered proteins frequently achieve RMSDs below 0.5 ppm, compared to ~1 ppm for folded proteins. In HEWL crystal simulations, B-factors from ff14SB align more closely with experiment than those from CHARMM36, ff14ipq, or ff99SB.

The paper builds upon foundational and contemporary works in force field development and NMR/crystallographic analysis. It references early parameterizations (Jorgensen et al. [20], Cornell et al. [21], MacKerell et al. [22]), modern torsional corrections (Lindorff-Larsen et al. [29], Best et al. [30], Maier et al. [33], Oostenbrink et al. [34], Tian et al. [35]), and benchmarking studies (Beauchamp et al. [44], Robustelli et al. [81], Lindorff-Larsen et al. [184]). It cites methodological standards for Karplus coefficients (Vögeli et al. [169], Li et al. [176], Lee et al. [175]), RDC analysis (Bax et al. [187–189], Zweckstetter [197]), NOE interpretation (Neuhaus & Williamson [203], Vögeli [204]), spin relaxation (Redfield [210], Lipari & Szabo [216, 217]), and PRE modeling (Solomon & Bloembergen [347]). It also points toward machine learning applications in protein dynamics (Janson & Feig [42], Brownless et al. [43]) and validation frameworks (van Gunsteren et al. [104, 105]).

This review does not address DFT shielding calculations, geometric kernel construction, or rank-2 tensor preservation, so it does not provide direct methodological or numerical anchors for the thesis calibration pipeline. Its primary value lies in documenting curated experimental datasets and calculation protocols that could serve as external validation targets for a shielding-based kernel system. The paper emphasizes that empirical chemical shift predictors carry baseline offsets and sequence-specific errors, and that direct comparison of simulated and experimental observables requires careful handling of conformational averaging and predictor uncertainty. For the thesis, this work supports the broader context of benchmarking protein ensembles against solution-phase NMR data, but the calibration of geometric kernels against DFT shielding values remains an independent methodological step not covered here.
