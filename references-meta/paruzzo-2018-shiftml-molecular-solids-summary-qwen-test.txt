Paruzzo, F. M., Hofstetter, A., Musil, F., De, S., Ceriotti, M. & Emsley, L. (2018). Chemical shifts in molecular solids by machine learning. Nature Communications, 9, 4501. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-018-06972-x. 10 pages. 74 references. Funding: Swiss National Science Foundation Grant no. 200021_160112; NCCR MARVEL (SNF); ERC Horizon 2020 Grant 677013-HBMAP; EPFL Scientific IT and Application Support Center.

This is a research paper that develops and benchmarks a machine learning protocol for predicting isotropic chemical shifts in organic molecular solids. The authors argue that local atomic environments, rather than long-range periodicity, govern chemical shifts, and that a model trained on density functional theory (DFT) shifts can substitute for expensive GIPAW calculations in NMR crystallography. The intended audience is computational chemists and solid-state NMR practitioners.

The model employs Gaussian process regression with a multi-kernel combination of smooth overlap of atomic positions (SOAP) descriptors. Training uses 2,000 structures (CSD-2k, ~185,000 environments) selected via farthest point sampling from a 61,000-structure CSD subset. DFT shifts are computed using GIPAW with Quantum Espresso for the training set and CASTEP for polymorphs, referenced to σ_ref = 30.8 ppm (1H) and 169.5 ppm (13C), derived from linear regression on cocaine. A combined SOAP kernel sums contributions from cut-off radii of 2 to 7 Å with weights 256, 128, 32, 8, 8, and 1. Symmetry-equivalent environments are removed, and cross-validation prunes 0.3% of training environments as outliers. Predictions are generated using the glosim2 package.

On a random 500-structure test set, the predicted shifts correlate with DFT values, yielding r² of 0.97 for 1H, 0.99 for 13C, 0.99 for 15N, and 0.99 for 17O, capturing the signal across the test set with RMSEs of 0.49, 4.3, 13.3, and 17.7 ppm, respectively. For polymorphs of cocaine (86 atoms) and AZD8329, 1H RMSEs are 0.37 and 0.46 ppm. Heteronuclear RMSEs for AZD8329 reach 54.7 ppm for 17O, attributed to sparse training data for its C–O…H–N/C–O…H–O hydrogen-bonded dimer motif. Against experimental shifts for six molecules (68 data points), the model achieves an RMSE of 0.39 ppm and r² of 0.99, matching the DFT-experimental RMSE of 0.38 ppm. Prediction time scales linearly with atom count, requiring less than one CPU minute for 86-atom systems and under six minutes for structures up to 1,584 atoms, compared to an estimated 16 CPU years for GIPAW.

The work builds on GIPAW formalisms (Pickard & Mauri, 2001; Yates et al., 2007), SOAP descriptors (Bartók et al., 2013; De et al., 2016), and Gaussian process regression (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006). It extends prior machine learning applications to chemical shifts, including neural network approaches for gas/liquid phases (Rupp et al., 2015; Cuny et al., 2016) and a single prior study on silicas (Cuny et al., 2016). It engages with the NMR crystallography literature that couples crystal structure prediction with GIPAW calculations (Baias et al., 2013; Salager et al., 2009, 2010) and protein shift prediction methods (Shen & Bax, 2007; Wishart et al., 1997). The authors point toward future extensions to halides, network solids, and potential training on experimental databases.

The paper demonstrates that local environment descriptors can capture the signal of chemical shifts with high correlation, validating the premise that shielding information is encoded in short- and medium-range atomic arrangements. However, the model predicts scalar isotropic shifts rather than rank-2 shielding tensors, and it targets molecular solids rather than protein backbones or side chains. The SOAP-GPR framework and multi-kernel weighting differ from the thesis’s geometric kernel construction. The use of DFT as a training proxy aligns with the thesis’s calibration strategy, but the divergence in target property (scalar δ vs tensor) and system class limits direct methodological transfer. The work supports the broader feasibility of machine learning for NMR parameters but does not address tensor preservation or protein-specific geometric kernels.
