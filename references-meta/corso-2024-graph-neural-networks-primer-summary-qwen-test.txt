This Primer provides a practical introduction to graph neural networks (GNNs) for students and practitioners in the life and physical sciences. It frames GNNs as deep learning models that learn functions over graph-structured data by iteratively aggregating information from neighbouring nodes. The authors explicitly position the work as an accessible overview rather than a comprehensive theoretical survey, directing readers to refs. 4 and 5 for exhaustive foundations. The text covers the standard message-passing framework, architectural variations, implementation considerations, applications, and fundamental limitations.

The central formalism is the message-passing layer, where node representations are updated by aggregating transformed vectors from neighbours, typically parameterized by feedforward networks and stochastic gradient descent. The authors illustrate the framework using a molecular property prediction task: classifying HIV inhibition on the Open Graph Benchmark dataset containing 40,000 molecules. The model uses an embedding layer, four message-passing layers, sum pooling, and a linear classifier. Data splitting employs scaffold splits to simulate distribution shifts. The paper details architectural choices including permutation invariance/equivariance, locality bias, and SE(3) invariance for 3D geometric graphs. It also covers efficient sparse implementations with O(|E|) complexity and libraries such as PyTorch Geometric, Deep Graph Library, Chemprop, and e3nn.

In the worked example, the GNN achieves 77.9% ROC-AUC on the validation set and 74.5% on the test set after early stopping, compared to 70.5% for a shallow feedforward network on Morgan fingerprints. The authors note that standard message-passing GNNs are bounded by the Weisfeiler–Leman isomorphism test, limiting their ability to distinguish certain non-isomorphic graphs. They identify oversmoothing (node features converging across layers) and oversquashing (bottlenecks in information flow between distant nodes) as persistent architectural constraints. Interpretability methods such as GNNExplainer and surrogate models are discussed, alongside uncertainty quantification challenges in transductive and inductive settings.

The Primer builds on foundational GNN formulations by Gori et al. (2005), Scarselli et al. (2008), and Gilmer et al. (2017), and situates modern architectures within the expressivity framework established by Xu et al. (2019) and Morris et al. (2019). It references Veličković et al. (2017) for attention mechanisms, Corso et al. (2020) for principal neighbourhood aggregation, and Schütt et al. (2017) and Geiger & Smidt (2022) for SE(3)-equivariant models. Applications cite Stokes et al. (2020) for antibiotic discovery, Jin et al. (2018, 2020) for molecular generation, and Dauparas et al. (2022) and Corso et al. (2023) for protein and ligand structure prediction. Theoretical discussions draw on Alon & Yahav (2021) for oversquashing and Rusch et al. (2023) for oversmoothing.

This Primer outlines the general GNN landscape, message-passing formalism, and geometric invariance requirements, which provide context for any deep learning component in the kernel calibration pipeline. It confirms that sparse implementations maintain O(|E|) complexity and that SE(3) equivariance is standard for 3D structural data. However, the paper does not address tensor-valued outputs, shielding-specific physical features, or calibration against DFT reference values. It does not discuss rank preservation or kernel-to-shielding mapping. The limitations noted—oversmoothing, oversquashing, and expressivity bounds under the Weisfeiler–Leman test—apply to general graph representations but do not directly constrain the geometric kernel construction or the rank-2 tensor preservation strategy. The work serves as background on GNN capabilities and failure modes rather than a methodological ancestor for the shielding calibration framework.
