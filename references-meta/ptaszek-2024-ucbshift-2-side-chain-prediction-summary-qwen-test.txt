Ptaszek, A. L.; Li, J.; Konrat, R.; Platzer, G.; Head-Gordon, T. UCBShift 2.0: Bridging the Gap from Backbone to Side Chain Protein Chemical Shift Prediction for Protein Structures. J. Am. Chem. Soc. 2024, 146 (46), 31733–31745. DOI: 10.1021/jacs.4c10474. 27 pages, 88 references. This research paper extends the UCBShift 1.0 algorithm to predict side-chain carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen chemical shifts for aqueous proteins. The authors argue that coupling a homology-based transfer module with a machine learning module trained on static crystal structure features yields higher predictive reliability than existing predictors such as SHIFTX2. The work targets computational NMR and structural biology audiences, positioning the method as a practical tool for validating protein structures and probing conformational dynamics.

The UCBShift 2.0 architecture comprises two components: UCBShift-Y, which transfers experimental shifts from the BMRB database via sequence (BLAST) and structural (mTM-align, TM score >0.8, RMSD <1.75 Å) alignment, and UCBShift-X, which employs an Extra Tree Regressor followed by a Random Forest Regressor trained on engineered features extracted from protonated X-ray structures. Features include BLOSUM62 scores, backbone and side-chain dihedral angles (sine/cosine), hydrogen bond descriptors (DSSP model), polynomial transforms, S² order parameters, accessible surface area, secondary structure, B-factors, hydrophobicity, pH, protonation indicators, electric field effects (using Amber force field charges), and ring current contributions calculated via the Haigh–Mallion model. The training set comprises 851 protein structures, filtered for outliers (>6 ppm for H, >12 ppm for C) and corrected for Asn/Gln amide proton stereospecific assignments based on established chemical shift difference rules.

UCBShift 2.0 achieves average mean absolute errors of 0.80 ppm for carbon, 0.16 ppm for hydrogen, and 0.99 ppm for nitrogen. Pearson correlation coefficients (R) range from −0.25 to 0.86 across atom types, with RMSE values between 0.17 and 1.91 ppm. The Y-module accounts for approximately 50–60% of the performance gain over SHIFTX2, reducing MAE by 0.5 ppm for carbons and 0.2 ppm for hydrogens when homology is available. Feature importance analysis indicates that ring currents dominate side-chain hydrogen predictions (>20% importance for HB, HB2/3, HG2/3/12/13, HD2/3, HE21), while hydrogen bonding features govern N-bound hydrogens. B-factors, S² parameters, hydrophobicity, pH, and electric fields contribute negligibly. On a low-homology test set (≤50% identity, 59 proteins), UCBShift maintains superior performance. For a subset of 20 HB atoms in Ile/Val involved in CH⋯π interactions (distance ≤3.7 Å, shifts <1 ppm), UCBShift-X yields an MAE of 0.36 ppm, outperforming the full UCBShift model (0.52 ppm) and SHIFTX2 (0.78 ppm).

The work builds directly on UCBShift 1.0 (Li et al., 2020) and SHIFTX2 (Han et al., 2011), while incorporating the Haigh–Mallion ring current formalism (Haigh & Mallion, 1979) and Amber force field parameters (Case et al., 2021) for feature engineering. It references foundational backbone predictors such as SPARTA+ (Shen & Bax, 2010) and PPM (Li & Brüschweiler, 2012). The authors point toward applications in validating AlphaFold2 structures (Jumper et al., 2021), automated assignment via ARTINA and NMRtist (Klukowski et al., 2022, 2023), and probing protein dynamics through random coil index and relaxation dispersion methods (Berjanskii & Wishart, 2008; Hansen et al., 2010). The bibliography also cites studies on CH⋯π interactions (Brandl et al., 2001; Platzer et al., 2020) and protein-ligand binding poses (Platzer et al., 2024; Beier et al., 2024).

This paper demonstrates that scalar chemical shifts correlate with static geometric descriptors, particularly ring currents and hydrogen bonds, when processed through ensemble tree models. The explicit use of the Haigh–Mallion ring current model and an electrostatic term based on Amber charges provides a concrete reference for how classical physical descriptors are operationalized as input features in modern shift prediction. The finding that ring currents capture >20% of the variance for side-chain hydrogens, and that UCBShift-X outperforms the homology-transfer module for extreme shielding cases, suggests that physics-based geometric kernels can capture non-local environmental signals that pure sequence alignment misses. However, the study operates entirely at the scalar shift level and does not address rank-2 tensor preservation, DFT calibration, or the decomposition of shielding into distinct tensor components. Its relevance to our framework lies in validating the predictive utility of ring-current and electrostatic features derived from static coordinates, while highlighting the limitations of static structures in capturing solution-phase conformational averaging.
