Agarwal, A., Barnes, J.A., Fletcher, J.L., McGlinchey, M.J. & Sayer, B.G. (1977) "Ring currents, local anisotropy, and the problem of the out-of-plane protons: a reinvestigation of the nuclear magnetic resonance spectrum of [10]-paracyclophane." Canadian Journal of Chemistry 55, 2575–2581. Received 28 July 1976; revision received 23 March 1977. 7 pages, 28 references. Department of Chemistry, McMaster University, Hamilton, Ontario. Sample of [10]-paracyclophane gifted by Prof. N.L. Allinger (Georgia). National Research Council of Canada support.

A reinvestigation of the ¹H NMR spectrum of [10]-paracyclophane using 220 MHz with homonuclear decoupling. The molecule is a geometric calibration system for ring-current models — a decamethylene bridge connects the para positions of a benzene ring, holding five distinguishable methylene environments (α, β, γ, δ, ε) at known positions above and near the aromatic plane. The paper's goal is to quantitatively test Johnson-Bovey and Haigh-Mallion ring-current models for out-of-plane protons and partition the observed shifts into ring-current versus local-anisotropic contributions from the ring carbons.

Method. 220 MHz ¹H NMR in CDCl₃ at 18°C; homonuclear decoupling resolves five methylene environments. Molecular geometry from Allinger molecular mechanics (MM2-era), validated by Karplus-relation analysis of vicinal coupling constants. Local anisotropic contributions from ring carbons computed via the current-in-a-loop expression σ_ii^H = G σ_ii^C using Grant's solid-state ¹³C shielding tensors (Barfield-Grant-Ikenberry 1975). Ring-current contributions from both Johnson-Bovey (two-loop, adjustable separation S) and Haigh-Mallion (quantum mechanical) tabulations, with parameters fit to experimental data after local-anisotropy subtraction.

Results. Observed shifts: α=2.62, β=1.54, γ=1.08, δ=0.73, ε=0.51 ppm. Calculated ring-current contributions in Table 1 differ substantially between JB and HM tabulations (α 1.66 JB vs 1.60 HM, but β 1.55 JB vs 1.36 HM, and farther protons diverge more). The key finding is methodological: the traditional two-loop separation parameter S in JB is unnecessary — a single loop at the ring plane (S=0) gives the best experimental correlation (r=0.9928), compared with S=1.28 Å (r=0.8883) or S=1.63 Å (r=0.7634). Ring-current accounts for about 65% of the observed incremental shift; the remaining 35% is from local anisotropic contributions of neighboring ring carbons. HM and JB intensity factors differ by ~2.6 (ΔRC_JB = 2.60 ΔRC_HM), consistent with later calibrations.

Relations to our corpus. Not foundational for us. Cites Pople 1956 (ref 4), Waugh & Fessenden 1957/1958 (ref 6), Johnson & Bovey 1958 (ref 8), McWeeny 1958 (ref 9), Haigh & Mallion 1971 and 1972 (refs 10, 11), Barfield-Grant-Ikenberry 1975 (ref 24, the ¹³C shielding tensors used for local anisotropy). All pointers already on the BL acquisition list. Dailey 1965 (ref 28) — reference for the ~60% ring-current contribution figure.

Implications for our work. Three useful points. (i) Out-of-plane proton shifts near an aromatic ring partition roughly 65% ring current / 35% local bond anisotropy. Our library separates these via BS/HM ring-current kernels and McConnell bond-anisotropy kernels respectively; this paper gives an experimental partition to calibrate against. (ii) The two-loop-versus-single-loop distinction in Johnson-Bovey is numerically irrelevant when local anisotropy is handled separately — a single loop at the ring plane (S=0) fits experiment as well or better. Worth checking which convention our `BiotSavartResult` uses and whether the choice is load-bearing. (iii) Concrete numerical benchmarks: five methylenes at known (ρ, z) positions above a benzene ring, with measured shifts and model predictions from both JB and HM. Useful as a sanity-check benchmark for our library's BS and HM outputs at constrained geometries — reconstructable from Allinger mechanics on [10]-paracyclophane. Technical-corpus material, not a bib candidate.
