Li, D.-W., & Bruschweiler, R. (2012). PPM: a side-chain and backbone chemical shift predictor for the assessment of protein conformational ensembles. *J Biomol NMR*, 54, 257–265. DOI: 10.1007/s10858-012-9668-8. 9 pages, 58 references. Supported by NSF grant MCB-0918362. Acknowledges A. Palmer.

Research paper introducing PPM, a chemical shift predictor designed for evaluating protein conformational ensembles and refining molecular mechanics force fields. The authors argue that experimental chemical shifts represent Boltzmann-weighted averages over conformational substates, making them sensitive probes of solution dynamics. PPM is parameterized directly against extended molecular dynamics (MD) ensembles rather than static crystal structures, aiming to separate dynamic averaging from geometric descriptor contributions.

The predictor computes shifts from 35 proteins (PDB/BMRB codes in Table S1) using 100 ns explicit-solvent MD trajectories generated with GROMACS 4 and the ff99SB_uw(g24;CS) force field augmented with ILDN side-chain corrections. Coordinates were sampled every 100 ps, yielding 1000 conformers per protein. Geometric descriptors include ring current (Haigh–Mallion), magnetic anisotropy (Case–McConnell), electric field, hydrogen bonding, dihedral, and sequence effects. Parameters were determined via linear least-squares regression against experimental shifts averaged over the MD ensembles. To prevent overfitting, 20% of data points were held out for validation across 1,000 iterations. For methyl protons, only ring current, magnetic anisotropy, and a conformation-independent offset term contributed significantly to the fit.

PPM predicts methyl-proton shifts with MD-ensemble RMSDs ranging from 0.12 to 0.22 ppm, with correlation coefficients between 0.68 and 0.91. Fitted ring-current prefactors are 5.817, 4.887, 4.860, 5.566, and 5.961 ppm Å for Phe, Tyr, His, Trp-5, and Trp-6, respectively. Magnetic anisotropy prefactors for backbone OCN, side-chain OCN (Asn/Gln), OCO (Glu/Asp), and NCN (Arg) groups are -4.479, -0.926, -1.692, and -0.408 ppm Å³. Backbone shifts for Ca, Cb, C', N, and HN yield RMSDs of 0.49 to 2.91 ppm across fitting and validation sets. The method requires ~30 s per 100-residue protein on a single core. Predictions from MD ensembles consistently outperform static-structure inputs for PPM and Shifts, while CH3Shift shows no ensemble benefit.

The work builds on Haigh and Mallion (1972, 1979) for ring-current geometry, Osapay and Case (1991) and McConnell (1957) for magnetic anisotropy, and Sahakyan et al. (2011) for side-chain anisotropy models. It extends the ensemble-parameterization strategy of Lehtivarjo et al. (2009, 2012) but uses longer trajectories and a force field optimized against NMR observables (Li and Bruschweiler 2010a, 2011; Long et al. 2011). The authors benchmark against Shifts, ShiftX/ShiftX2, SPARTA/SPARTA?, CamShift, 4DSPOT, and CH3Shift, noting that SPARTA? achieves higher accuracy on static structures due to neural-network training on 580 proteins, whereas PPM prioritizes physical interpretability and ensemble consistency.

The paper does not employ DFT shielding calculations or rank-2 tensor kernels; it relies on scalar empirical fits to geometric descriptors. However, its explicit separation of ensemble averaging from prefactor calibration aligns with the thesis’s approach of fitting geometric kernels to averaged observables. The reported correlation coefficients indicate that the physical descriptors capture the dominant signal in methyl shifts, consistent with the thesis’s framing of prediction as signal capture rather than pointwise agreement. The fitted prefactors for ring current and magnetic anisotropy provide quantitative benchmarks for the relative weight of these geometric contributions. The study confirms that extended MD sampling improves ensemble-averaged shift prediction, supporting the use of trajectory-based calibration in the thesis. No direct methodological overlap exists beyond the ensemble-parameterization philosophy and the emphasis on physical-chemical descriptors.
