Chiheb Ben Mahmoud, Louise A. M. Rosset, Jonathan R. Yates, and Volker L. Deringer (2024). Graph-neural-network predictions of solid-state NMR parameters from spherical tensor decomposition. arXiv:2412.15063v1 [cond-mat.mtrl-sci], 13 pages, 89 references. Funding: Swiss National Science Foundation (SNSF) grant 217837; UK Research and Innovation Frontier Research grant EP/X016188/1; EPSRC grant EP/T026642/1 (Soprano); UKCP/ARCHER2 computational support.

This is a methodological benchmarking study presenting a machine learning framework for predicting rank-2 solid-state NMR tensors—magnetic shielding (σ) and electric field gradient (V)—using the NequIP graph neural network. The authors argue that representing these tensors as spherical harmonics enables robust equivariant learning and systematically compare two decomposition strategies against GIPAW-DFT reference data. The work targets researchers seeking to accelerate anisotropic property prediction for disordered and dynamic systems without repeated first-principles calculations.

The authors implement two spherical tensor decomposition schemes within NequIP: irreducible spherical decomposition (ISD), which projects σ and V into independent ℓ = 0, 1, 2 components, and a tensor product (TP) representation, which constructs targets from linear combinations of tensor products of rank ℓ ≥ 1 spherical harmonics. Training uses 1,000 amorphous SiO2 structures (2.0–2.7 g cm⁻³) generated via melt–quench–anneal molecular dynamics with the CHIK and GAP interatomic potentials, employing three quench rates (10^11, 10^12, and 10^13 K s⁻¹). The dataset is split into 800 training, 50 internal validation, and 150 test structures. GIPAW-DFT calculations employ the PBE functional, 900 eV plane-wave cutoff, and 0.03 Å⁻¹ k-point spacing. Hyperparameters are optimized via Bayesian optimization (XPOT) on 100 structures. Predictions are evaluated using root mean square error (RMSE) and normalized RMSE (%RMSE), defined as RMSE divided by the standard deviation of the target quantity.

The ISD model achieves the lowest prediction errors across silicon and oxygen sites. For silicon, %RMSE values for σ^(0), σ^(1), and σ^(2) remain below 20%, though the asymmetry (η_σ) and span (ℱ_σ) parameters show higher errors due to sensitivity to tensor shape. Oxygen σ^(1) yields %RMSE of 26.14% (ISD) and 31.70% (TP), corresponding to 0.80 and 0.97 ppm. The TP model requires approximately 2.16 million trainable parameters compared to 6.22 million for ISD, yet captures similar signal fidelity. Learning curves show algebraic error decay with training set size, with oxygen σ^(1) plateauing near 30% %RMSE at 500 structures. Simulated static 1D and MAS spectra from ML tensors correlate with GIPAW references, reproducing experimental α–β cristobalite peak separations within 0.3 ppm, though crystalline β-phase extrapolations show a 19 ppm positional discrepancy.

The work builds on prior GNN applications to isotropic NMR shielding in proteins and glasses (Kwon et al. 2020; Han et al. 2022; Bånkestad et al. 2024), and explicitly extends Venetos et al. (2023) and Harper et al. (2024) by demonstrating that learning full tensors via spherical decomposition outperforms specialized models for anisotropy parameters. It leverages the NequIP architecture (Batzner et al. 2022) and e3nn library (Geiger & Smidt 2022) for equivariant message passing, while situating its methodology within the broader MLIP literature (Behler & Parrinello 2007; Bartók et al. 2010; Drautz 2019; Schütt et al. 2019) and NMR tensor conventions (Harris et al. 2008; Mason 1993; Haeberlen 1976). The authors also reference foundational work on tensor field networks (Thomas et al. 2018) and Clebsch–Gordan coupling formalisms for harmonic decomposition.

This paper demonstrates that equivariant GNNs can preserve rank-2 tensor structure during learning, with ISD providing a parameter-efficient pathway to correlate spherical components with DFT references. The explicit separation of ℓ = 0, 1, 2 channels aligns with the thesis requirement to maintain tensor rank rather than collapsing to isotropic averages. However, the study focuses on SiO2 polymorphs and hypothetical zeolites rather than protein backbones, and does not address geometric kernel construction or per-element complexity scaling. The correlation between ML-predicted spherical components and GIPAW targets confirms that signal capture is feasible for anisotropic descriptors, but the higher errors for oxygen σ^(1) and shape-sensitive parameters (η_σ, ℱ_σ) suggest that calibration strategies will need to weight tensor components differently. The dynamic trajectory application indicates that ML tensors can track phase transitions without repeated DFT, supporting future integration with protein conformational sampling.
