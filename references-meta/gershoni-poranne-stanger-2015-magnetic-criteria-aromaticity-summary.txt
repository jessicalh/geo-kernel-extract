A critical review of magnetic-based criteria used to diagnose aromaticity and antiaromaticity in molecules. Three strands are surveyed: (i) ¹H-NMR chemical shifts via the ring current model (RCM), (ii) nucleus-independent chemical shifts (NICS) and their many variants, and (iii) current density analysis (CDA) using ab initio methods. The review argues that magnetic criteria are necessary but not sufficient for defining aromaticity, and walks through the strengths and failure modes of each class of indicator.

Central tools and arguments. Aromatic systems show diatropic (clockwise) ring currents under an external field; antiaromatic 4n π systems show paratropic (counter-clockwise) currents. NICS computes the negative-of-shielding at a ghost atom placed at a chosen location — NICS(0) at the ring center, NICS(1) at 1 Å above, etc. Negative NICS indicates aromaticity. Because NICS(0) is contaminated by σ-framework effects, many refinements were developed: dissected NICS methods (LMO-NICS, CMO-NICS, NCS-NICS) that separate σ from π contributions via localized molecular orbitals; NICS_zz (the z-component of the shielding tensor, which isolates π-ring-current effects for planar systems); NICS_πzz (the most refined index, combining π-only and zz-component decompositions); the σ-Only Model of Fallah-Bagher-Shaidaei et al. 2006; and the multi-dimensional NICS-Scan and NICS-XY-Scan methods that give 1D/2D spatial profiles rather than single-point values. CDA methods visualize current density directly. CTOCD-DZ (ipsocentric) and CTOCD-PZ methods (Lazzeretti-Zanasi-Fowler) address gauge-origin issues. GIMIC (Jusélius-Sundholm-Gauss 2004) integrates current flow to produce quantitative ring-current strengths. ACID (Herges-Geuenich 2001) generates gauge-invariant scalar fields as isosurfaces.

Intellectual narrative. Magnetic aromaticity criteria descend from Pople's 1956 point-dipole ring current model, with antecedents in Pauling-Wheland 1933 (diamagnetic susceptibility) and the Hückel molecular orbital theory of the 1930s. Schleyer and co-workers' 1996 NICS paper (cited 2670 times at the time of this review) launched the modern computational era. Subsequent work refined NICS away from σ-contamination (Schleyer 1997 NICS(1); Steiner-Fowler dissected NICS; Bohmann-Weinhold-Farrar NCS-NICS). CDA methods emerged in parallel from Lazzeretti & Zanasi (CTOCD-DZ/PZ and its DZ2/PZ2 variants), Jusélius-Sundholm (GIMIC), and Herges-Geuenich (ACID). Roger Mitchell's group at Victoria pioneered the experimental use of NMR chemical shifts in annulene and fused-ring probe systems as direct aromaticity reporters.

Implications for our work, widely considered. This review clarifies that our BS and HM kernels are RCM-based (the simplest of the three frameworks); NICS and CDA are more sophisticated alternatives we do not use because they require per-evaluation QM calculations, while our kernels are closed-form geometric functions. Two direct thesis-relevant points: (i) the review documents that NICS_zz (the z-component of the shielding tensor at a ghost atom) is more accurate than NICS_iso for distinguishing aromatic from antiaromatic systems, because it isolates the π-ring-current contribution — a direct analog of our T2 focus over T0 in the T0/T1/T2 decomposition. Independent precedent from the aromaticity literature for the "tensor component matters more than the trace" argument. (ii) The σ-Only Model and dissected-NICS methods (LMO/CMO) offer alternative σ/π separation done directly from localized molecular orbitals — our kernel-based approach does not do this; the trade is that our kernels are computationally cheap and scale to whole proteins, where per-atom QM is infeasible. Not directly actionable for Stage 1 or Stage 2 implementation, but a good citation for thesis introduction context: magnetic-response interpretation of aromaticity is a major and still-evolving subfield, of which our work is one practical application at the protein scale.
