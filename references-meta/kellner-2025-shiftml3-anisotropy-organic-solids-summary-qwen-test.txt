A deep learning model for chemical shieldings in molecular organic solids including anisotropy. arXiv:2506.13146v3 [physics.chem-ph], 14 Sep 2025. 18 pages (including supporting information). 100 references. Supported by Swiss National Science Foundation Grant No. 200020_212046, NCCR MARVEL (grant 182892), and ERC Horizon 2020 Grant No. 101001890-FIAMMA.

This research paper introduces ShiftML3, a machine learning model designed to predict full chemical shielding tensors, including chemical shift anisotropy (CSA), for molecular organic solids. The authors frame the work as a response to the limitations of previous models that predicted only isotropic shifts, arguing that tensorial predictions are necessary for structure elucidation via CSA measurements. The target audience comprises computational chemists and NMR crystallographers. The paper positions itself as a methodological advance in deep learning for solid-state NMR, aiming to deliver DFT-level accuracy at a fraction of the computational cost.

The model employs an ensemble of eight nanoPET architectures, an unconstrained graph neural network that learns rotational equivariance through training-time augmentations rather than hard constraints. Training data comprises 14,146 organic solid structures (1.4 million atomic environments) containing H, C, N, O, F, Na, Mg, P, S, Cl, K, and Ca, with shielding tensors computed at the GIPAW/PBE level. Local environments are encoded using a 5 Å radial cutoff. The network predicts irreducible spherical tensor components (λ=0 scalar, λ=1 pseudovector, λ=2 proper 5-vector) via a single message-passing sequence with two message-passing layers, each containing two attention layers, and a token dimensionality of 192. Uncertainty estimates are derived from the committee spread and calibrated using a global, input-independent correction factor.

On the CSD-test set, ShiftML3 correlates with GIPAW reference isotropic shieldings with R² values ranging from 0.68 (²³Na) to 1.00 (¹³C, ¹⁷O). RMSEs against DFT are 0.43 ppm (¹H), 2.32 ppm (¹³C), 10.41 ppm (¹⁵N), and 11.45 ppm (¹⁷O), representing up to a two-fold improvement over ShiftML2 for isotropic shieldings of nuclei with Z > 1. Tensor component predictions show R² values of 0.98–1.00 for ¹H, ¹³C, ¹⁵N, ¹⁷O, and ¹⁹F principal components, with RMSEs of 0.85, 3.88, 15.62, 16.79, and 8.65 ppm, respectively. Against experimental benchmarks, isotropic RMSEs are 0.53 ppm (¹H), 2.44 ppm (¹³C), and 7.24 ppm (¹⁵N), closely tracking DFT errors of 0.49, 2.34, and 5.85 ppm. CSA principal component predictions yield an experimental RMSE of 5.2 ppm versus 5.85 ppm for DFT. Inference scales linearly with system size, requiring 41 seconds on a consumer laptop for benchmark sets compared to ~80 hours for DFT.

The work extends the ShiftML lineage [45, 49], which previously used kernel ridge regression with SOAP descriptors on isotropic shifts. It builds on PET architecture developments [61, 73] and uncertainty quantification strategies [57, 83–89]. The authors compare performance against prior neural network models trained on smaller datasets [46, 87, 99], noting that direct comparisons are limited by differing DFT convergence parameters and pseudopotential choices. The paper references foundational NMR crystallography methods [1–6, 13, 14] and DFT shielding theory [7, 8, 11, 12], while pointing toward applications in protein solid-state NMR, as demonstrated by testing on the 1PGA crystal structure.

ShiftML3 demonstrates that full rank-2 shielding tensors can be learned directly from atomic coordinates with high correlation to DFT references, capturing the structural signal underlying chemical shift anisotropy. The ensemble framework and uncertainty calibration provide a template for evaluating prediction reliability, though the unconstrained GNN relies on rotational augmentation rather than exact equivariance. The paper does not address protein-specific environments, hydrogen-bond networks, or explicit geometric kernels, and its training set focuses on small-molecule organic crystals. Its treatment of tensor preservation and calibration may inform validation protocols for kernel-based shielding models, particularly in assessing how well structural features map to rank-2 tensor components. The linear scaling and low inference cost suggest potential utility for larger systems, but the model’s applicability to biomolecular contexts remains untested.
