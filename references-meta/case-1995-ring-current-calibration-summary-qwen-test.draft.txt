Case, D. A. (1995). Calibration of ring-current effects in proteins and nucleic acids. Journal of Biomolecular NMR, 6, 341-346. DOI not provided in the text. 6 pages. 31 references. Supported by NIH Grant GM45811. Acknowledges Richard Bryce for computational assistance and D. R. Salahub, V. G. Malkin, and O. L. Malkina for providing the DEMON density functional program.

This paper is a calibration study that reassesses empirical ring-current intensity factors and electrostatic parameters for aromatic residues in proteins and nucleic acids. The author argues that modern density functional theory calculations can serve as reliable benchmarks for secondary proton shifts, allowing long-used empirical formulas to be quantitatively refined. The work targets researchers who use chemical shift data to derive structural restraints or refine biomolecular models, positioning quantum calculations as a practical supplement to empirical and semiempirical parametrizations.

The methodology centers on 421 computed secondary proton shielding values obtained by placing a methane probe molecule at 8–12 distinct positions near ten aromatic ring systems (benzene, phenol, imidazole, imidazolium, indole, adenine, guanidine, uracil, thymine, cytosine). Interproton-to-ring distances span 2.9 Å to 5.5 Å, with geometries sampled from X-ray structure averages in the AMBER database. Shieldings were calculated using the DEMON program with the PW91 exchange-correlation functional, the IGLO-III basis set, and the IGLO gauge correction; Hartree-Fock/GIAO/6-31++G** calculations were run for comparison. Ring-current contributions were modeled using Johnson-Bovey and Haigh-Mallion geometric factors, while electrostatic polarization was parameterized via a Buckingham-type term proportional to the local electric field projected onto the C–H bond. Parameters were optimized using a nonlinear routine with jackknife resampling (10 iterations, 1/10 data omitted per iteration) to estimate parameter uncertainties.

The DFT-calculated secondary shifts correlate strongly with empirical predictions, indicating that the geometric models capture the shielding signal. For nucleic acid bases (207 shifts), Pearson r values are 0.943 (Johnson-Bovey) and 0.953 (Haigh-Mallion), with RMSEs of 0.097 and 0.088 ppm. For protein aromatic rings (171 shifts), r reaches 0.994 and 0.991, with RMSEs of 0.059 and 0.071 ppm. The electrostatic parameter A was determined to be -2.98 × 10⁻¹² esu⁻¹ (Johnson-Bovey) and -3.11 × 10⁻¹² esu⁻¹ (Haigh-Mallion). Optimized ring-current intensity factors are generally larger than prior semiempirical estimates; representative values include phenylalanine 1.27/1.46, tyrosine 1.10/1.24, histidine 1.40/1.35, tryptophan five-membered ring 1.02/1.32, and six-membered ring 1.27/1.24. Pyrimidine bases show consistently lower intensities (0.24–0.37) than purines (0.81–1.14). Absolute shieldings exhibit strong basis-set and gauge dependence, but secondary shifts converge within 0.2 ppm across methods when diffuse and polarization functions are included.

The work builds on the theoretical foundations of ring-current effects by Salem (1966), the empirical Johnson-Bovey (1958) and Haigh-Mallion (1980) models, and Buckingham’s (1960) electrostatic formulation. It extends prior intensity estimates from Giessner-Prettre and Pullman (1969, 1987) and empirical protein studies by Osapay and Case (1991, 1994) and Williamson and Asakura (1993). The paper points toward applications in structure refinement using shift-derived penalty functions (Csapay et al., 1994; Kuszewski et al., 1995) and acknowledges the growing role of quantum calculations in interpreting proton shifts (Wishart et al., 1991; Oldfield, 1995; Le et al., 1995).

For the thesis, this paper provides a benchmarked set of scalar ring-current intensity factors and an electrostatic coefficient that can anchor the calibration of the ring-current geometric kernel. The high correlation between empirical geometric models and DFT secondary shifts supports the thesis’s premise that geometric kernels capture the shielding signal, even if pointwise accuracy requires further refinement. The study confirms that electrostatic polarization contributes significantly to shift dispersion and should be included alongside ring-current terms. While the paper treats shifts as scalars and does not address tensor preservation or per-element dimensionality, its calibrated parameters offer a clear target for validating the thesis’s kernel-to-shielding pipeline. The robustness of secondary shifts against gauge and basis-set variations further justifies focusing on relative, geometry-dependent kernel outputs rather than absolute shieldings.
