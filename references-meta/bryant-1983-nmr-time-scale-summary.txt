Bryant, R.G. (1983) "The NMR Time Scale." Journal of Chemical Education 60(11), 933–935. Pedagogical article (3 pages), 6 references. Accessed via Birkbeck proxy.

Bryant argues that the phrase "the NMR time scale" — routinely used to say a chemical process is fast or slow relative to NMR's sensitivity — hides the fact that different NMR observables access different time ranges. Waving at "the time scale" without naming the observable produces statements that sound technical but are not meaningful.

The frame is the uncertainty relation Δν = h/(2πτ), linking a line's lifetime τ to the uncertainty Δν in its frequency. When a nucleus exchanges between two environments, the two resonance lines coalesce when the exchange rate 1/τ is comparable to the frequency separation Δν between them: τ_coalescence = (√2·πΔν)⁻¹. This is the mechanism behind chemical-shift averaging, and its critical feature for practice is that Δν is in Hz, not ppm — so the averaging timescale scales with the magnetic field, and the same exchange process can be "slow" at 60 MHz and "fast" at 300 MHz. Scalar couplings obey the same analysis with the coupling constant J replacing Δν. Relaxation rates are a separate regime: the longitudinal rate 1/T₁ depends on the correlation time τ_c of the interaction modulating nuclear spin energy levels, and field-cycling / NMRD techniques extend accessible τ_c down into the picosecond range.

A worked example: water protons in 0.80 mM aqueous MnCl₂ at 286 K, measured 0.01–35 MHz. Two inflection points in the T₁⁻¹ dispersion report the electron relaxation time (low frequency) and the metal-complex rotational correlation time τ_rot ≈ 44 ps (higher frequency). A table covers representative shift ranges and coalescence times for ¹H, ¹³C, ¹⁵N, ¹⁹F, ³¹P, ⁵⁹Co, and ¹⁹⁹Hg at 1.4 T and 7 T, spanning roughly 0.2 s for narrow shifts down to sub-microsecond for cobalt. Bryant's summary line: the "NMR time scale" actually spans days to picoseconds.

Relations to our corpus. None of the six cited references (Bovey; Kaplan & Fraenkel on chemically exchanging systems; Farrar & Becker on pulse/FT NMR; Hallenga & Koenig; Swift on paramagnetic molecules; Dwek on enzyme NMR) is currently in our corpus; none is load-bearing for our argument.

Implications for our work. This is the pedagogical anchor for why MD simulation and NMR measurement timescales do not trivially align. Chemical-shift averaging in proteins operates in the μs–ms range (shift differences 1–300 Hz); our 25 ns MD trajectories sample conformational substates that exchange much faster than this averaging window, which is the regime in which averaging computed shifts over MD frames is the correct operation. De Gortari et al. 2010 extend this logic to 5 μs; Bryant gives the framework for why longer MD runtimes help rather than just adding noise. Secondarily, the paper is the clean reference for distinguishing ps–ns relaxation-based motion probes (Lipari–Szabo order parameters, not yet in corpus) from chemical-shift averaging, which operates on a different timescale and reports different physics.
