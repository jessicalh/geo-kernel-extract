Sahakyan AB, Vranken WF, Cavalli A, Vendruscolo M (2011) Structure-based prediction of methyl chemical shifts in proteins. J Biomol NMR 50:331–346. DOI 10.1007/s10858-011-9524-2. 16 pages. Funding: Herchel Smith Foundation (A.B.S.); Leverhulme Trust, EMBO, Royal Society, BBSRC (M.V.); EU FP7 e-NMR grant 213010 (W.F.V.).

This paper is a research article introducing CH3Shift, a structure-based predictor for methyl 1H and 13C chemical shifts in proteins. It aims to extend chemical shift-driven structure determination from backbone nuclei to side chains by providing a differentiable, coordinate-based model that can be used as restraints in molecular dynamics. The authors frame the work as a phenomenological calibration against experimental data, targeting researchers developing chemical shift predictors and NMR structure determination protocols.

The method combines six terms in a single equation: rotameric, dihedral, ring current, magnetic anisotropy, electric field, and distance-based contributions. Parameters were fitted to CH3Shift-DB, a curated database of 17,873 stereospecifically assigned methyl records from X-ray structures at 2.0 Å resolution or better, filtered for protein-nucleic acid complexes, porphyrinic rings, and iron or cobalt atoms, and restricted to values within 2.5 standard deviations of the residue-specific mean. Coefficient fitting used least squares in R, while model structure was optimized via a Monte Carlo procedure (70,000 steps, Metropolis criterion) selecting term combinations based on leave-one-out fitting quality. Validation also included random training/test splits (5–30% test data, 250 replicates). Ring current effects use Haigh-Mallion geometric factors with a 6.5 Å cutoff, justified by Case (1995). Magnetic anisotropy applies McConnell’s axial symmetry approximation to peptide and side-chain moieties. Electric field terms use Coulomb monopole interactions with Amber03 partial charges. Distance terms employ polynomial expansions in r, r⁻¹, r⁻³, and r⁻⁶, with distances merged by atom type. Protonation states are fixed to standard conditions during fitting (acidic residues deprotonated, lysine and cysteine protonated, histidine protonated only at the δ position).

Prediction standard errors for 1H shifts range from 0.133 to 0.198 ppm across Ala, Thr, Val, Leu, and Ile methyl groups. 13C predictions show limited improvement over random coil averages, attributed to unmodeled isotope effects from the attached hydrogen. Leave-one-out correlations indicate the model captures the structural signal without claiming pointwise agreement. Ensemble analysis on ubiquitin (2NR2 with 144 conformers, 2K39 with 116, 1D3Z with 10) shows predicted shifts correlate with conformational proximity to the 1UBQ X-ray structure, and leucine 13C shift differences align with Mulder’s rotamer-based equation Δδ13C(δ1-δ2) = -5 + 10p_tr. Random coil values for all methyl types are tabulated.

The paper builds on Haigh and Mallion (1972, 1980) for ring current geometry, McConnell (1957) for magnetic anisotropy, Buckingham (1960) for electric field polarization, and Case (1995) for ring current distance cutoffs. It adopts distance-term formalism from Kohlhoff et al. (2009) and backbone shift predictors (Wishart et al. 1997; Xu and Case 2001; Meiler 2003; Shen and Bax 2007; Lehtivarjo et al. 2009). It points toward London et al. (2008), Mulder (2009), and Hansen et al. (2010) for rotamer-shift relationships and leucine conformational analysis.

The work implements phenomenological terms that parallel the thesis’s geometric kernels (ring current, magnetic anisotropy, electric field), but collapses them into scalar predictions calibrated against BMRB data rather than DFT shielding values. It does not address tensor preservation or per-element complexity, treating shifts as isotropic scalars. The Monte Carlo term selection and leave-one-out validation provide a methodological precedent for calibration stability, though the thesis relies on DFT-based calibration. The database curation criteria and rotamer-dependent modeling offer background for data handling, but the paper is not a direct ancestor of the T2-preserving kernel framework. It serves as a scalar calibration benchmark rather than a tensor methodological source.
