Papaleo, E., Camilloni, C., Teilum, K., Vendruscolo, M., & Lindorff-Larsen, K. (2018). Molecular dynamics ensemble refinement of the heterogeneous native state of NCBD using chemical shifts and NOEs. PeerJ, 6, e5125. DOI: 10.7717/peerj.5125. 24 pages. Funding: Novo Nordisk Foundation (Hallas-Møller stipend to K.L.-L.), Danish e-Infrastructure Cooperation HPC Grant 2013, PRACE Research Infrastructure Resource Curie.

This is a research paper applying dynamic ensemble refinement (DER) to the 59-residue Nuclear Coactivator Binding Domain (NCBD) of CBP. The authors aim to demonstrate that combining backbone chemical shifts and nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE) distance restraints with replica-averaged molecular dynamics (MD) can accurately capture the conformational heterogeneity of a highly dynamic protein. The study argues that standard NMR structures capture only an average conformation, whereas replica-averaged simulations can model the underlying ensemble, reconciling apparently contradictory NMR relaxation and chemical shift data. The intended audience includes computational biologists and NMR spectroscopists working on protein dynamics and ensemble determination.

The authors performed replica-averaged MD simulations using Gromacs 4.5 and PLUMED 1.3 with the CHARMM22* force field, comparing results against CHARMM22 and unbiased trajectories. Simulations employed simulated annealing cycles (304–454 K, 0.6 ns per cycle) with 1, 2, 4, or 8 replicas. Backbone chemical shifts (Cα, Cβ, Hα, C′, H, N) were restrained using CamShift predictions and a harmonic potential with a force constant of 5.2 kJ mol⁻¹ ppm⁻². NOEs (455 distance intervals, 46 long-range) were restrained via r⁻⁶ averaging over replicas. Convergence was assessed using Jensen–Shannon divergence and root mean square inner product (RMSIP). Cross-validation used side-chain methyl chemical shifts predicted by CH3Shift and PPM. Fast-timescale dynamics were probed via 28 × 50 ns unbiased runs, fitting N–H bond-vector autocorrelation functions to the Lipari–Szabo model to extract relaxation order parameters (S²_relaxation). Long-timescale flexibility was quantified as S²_chemicalshift from the ensemble aligned using THESEUS.

Chemical shift or NOE restraints alone failed to reproduce experimental side-chain methyl shifts or long-range NOEs, indicating insufficient structural accuracy. Combining both restraints with CHARMM22* and four replicas (CS-NOE-4) yielded the best agreement with independent methyl shift data. The ensemble maintains three α-helices but exhibits broad conformational distributions. The radius of gyration (13.7–14.9 Å) is lower than SAXS estimates (~16.5–18.8 Å), attributed to an ~8% unfolded population in experiments and residual force-field compaction. S²_relaxation values are high across the backbone, indicating nanosecond rigidity. S²_chemicalshift values are systematically lower, revealing millisecond-timescale flexibility in loops and the C-terminus. The difference averages 0.2 for backbone amides and 0.4 for methyl side chains. Both calculated S² sets correlate strongly with experimental relaxation data (Spearman ρ = 0.9 and 0.8).

The work extends the original DER framework of Lindorff-Larsen et al. (2005) by substituting relaxation order parameters with backbone chemical shifts and NOEs, following methodological developments by Camilloni et al. (2012, 2013a, 2013b) and Cavalli et al. (2013). It relies on the CHARMM22* force field validation of Piana, Lindorff-Larsen & Shaw (2011) and Lindorff-Larsen et al. (2012a). The authors position the approach alongside replica-averaged metainference (Bonomi et al., 2016) and Bayesian ensemble refinement (Hummer & Köfinger, 2015; MacCallum, Perez & Dill, 2015). The discussion points toward future force-field improvements (Best, 2017), SAXS interpretation methods (Hub, 2018), and enhanced sampling algorithms (Bonomi, Camilloni & Vendruscolo, 2016).

The paper demonstrates that replica-averaged MD can capture multi-timescale conformational heterogeneity when multiple NMR observables are combined with an accurate force field. The method relies on ensemble averaging to capture the signal of time-averaged experimental observables, a principle that parallels the thesis’s correlation-based calibration of geometric kernels. However, the study operates on backbone chemical shifts and NOEs rather than shielding tensors, and it does not engage with DFT calibration, geometric kernels, or rank-2 tensor preservation. It serves as a methodological precedent for ensemble refinement but does not directly inform the tensor calibration pipeline. Its primary relevance lies in validating the use of chemical shifts as structural restraints and illustrating how different NMR timescales probe distinct dynamical regimes.
