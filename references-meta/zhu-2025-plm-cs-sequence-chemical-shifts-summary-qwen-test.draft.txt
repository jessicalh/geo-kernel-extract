Zhu, Hu, Yang, and Chen (2025) propose a sequence-only chemical shift predictor in *Digital Discovery*, 4, 331–337. DOI: 10.1039/d4dd00367e. The communication spans 7 pages and cites 32 references. Code and datasets are hosted on GitHub/Zenodo. Funding is provided by the Fujian Provincial Natural Science Foundation (2024J01054), the Natural Science Foundation of Xiamen (3502Z202373005), and the National Natural Science Foundation of China (22161142024, 11975192, 12175189).

This communication presents PLM-CS, a method for predicting backbone nuclear magnetic resonance chemical shifts using only amino acid sequences. The authors position the work as a low-cost, structure-independent alternative to conventional programs that require high-resolution protein coordinates. The paper argues that pre-trained protein language models implicitly encode structural and evolutionary information sufficient to predict scalar chemical shifts, offering robustness when experimental or crystallographic structures are unavailable or mismatched with solution-state NMR conditions.

The architecture combines a frozen ESM2-650M encoder with a transformer-based predictor. The encoder converts sequences into 1280-dimensional amino acid-level vectors, which are projected to 512 dimensions with positional encoding and passed through two transformer encoder layers featuring eight attention heads and GELU activation. Separate models are trained for each of the six backbone atom types (Ca, Cb, C, Ha, H, N) using RMSE loss, Adam optimization, and Kaiming initialization. The training set comprises 2,359 proteins from RefDB (BMRB files processed via SHIFTX), with sequences truncated or padded to 512 residues.

On the SHIFTX test set (61 proteins with crystal structures), PLM-CS yields RMSE values of 1.10 ppm (Ca), 1.16 ppm (Cb), 1.06 ppm (C), 0.30 ppm (Ha), 0.40 ppm (H), and 2.48 ppm (N). On a custom solution-NMR test set (76 proteins), RMSE values increase to 1.32, 1.39, 1.28, 0.31, 0.41, and 2.71 ppm, respectively. Samples exceeding three times the mean RMSE are excluded as outliers. The model outperforms a GNN-based sequence-structure method and a one-hot linear baseline, but remains less accurate than structure-dependent programs like SHIFTX2 and SPARTA+. Ablation confirms the transformer predictor improves accuracy over direct ESM vector projection, while the heterogeneous Cb distribution limits its prediction precision relative to other backbone atoms.

The work builds on single-sequence protein language models, particularly ESM (Rives et al., 2021) and BERT (Devlin et al., 2018), and positions itself against structure-based predictors such as PROSHIFT (Meiler, 2003), SPARTA+ (Shen and Bax, 2010), SHIFTX2 (Han et al., 2011), and graph neural network approaches (Yang et al., 2021). It references RefDB (Zhang et al., 2003) and BMRB (Ulrich et al., 2007) for dataset construction, and cites AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021) and related accuracy studies (Fowler and Williamson, 2021, 2022) to contextualize crystal versus solution structure discrepancies. The paper points toward applications in peak assignment and spectral validation but does not engage with tensorial shielding formalisms.

This paper does not address shielding tensors, geometric kernels, or DFT calibration, and therefore does not directly inform the thesis’s rank-2 tensor preservation framework. It demonstrates that sequence-only models can capture chemical environment signals sufficient for scalar shift prediction, but with higher uncertainty than structure-dependent methods. For the thesis, it serves as a boundary case: it confirms that sequence information alone carries limited structural resolution compared to explicit geometric kernels, reinforcing the rationale for using coordinate-dependent tensor components. The reported RMSE values and outlier handling provide a baseline for evaluating structure-independent prediction limits, but no methodological transfer is warranted.
