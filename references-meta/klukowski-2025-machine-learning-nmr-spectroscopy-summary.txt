Received 14 May 2025, accepted 25 June 2025, published online 1 July 2025. 15 pages, extensive reference list. Open access under CC BY. Editor: David Neuhaus. ETH Zurich (Institute of Molecular Physical Science — Klukowski, Riek, Güntert), Goethe Frankfurt and Tokyo Metropolitan University (additional affiliations for Güntert). Funding: SNSF 10001393 (Klukowski), internal ETH (Riek), JSPS JP23K05660 (Güntert). No declared competing interests.

A comprehensive review of machine learning applications in NMR spectroscopy, covering recent developments across three major application areas: (i) automated analysis of experimental data (peak picking, chemical shift assignment, protein structure determination); (ii) structure/dynamics-property prediction (chemical shifts in proteins, small molecules, and molecular solids; scalar couplings; protein dynamics); (iii) spectrum reconstruction and quality enhancement (NUS reconstruction, virtual decoupling, denoising). Target audience is NMR practitioners with limited prior ML background; the review develops the method taxonomy (CNN, GNN, LSTM, kernel regression, Gradient Boosted Trees) alongside the applications. Figure 1 is a chronological map of all surveyed methods from 2000 through 2025, with method names colour-coded by application domain and modelling approach.

Coverage of structure-based chemical shift prediction (Section 3 and Table 3) is complete for our reconnaissance-predictor set: PROSHIFT (2003 FF-NN with 350 handcrafted features), SPARTA+ (2010 FF-NN, 113 features), ShiftX2 (2011 regression trees + sequence alignment), PPM_One (2015), UCBShift (2020 random forest + alignment, 847 proteins), UCBShift 2.0 (2024 random forest + sequence AND structure alignment, 1051 proteins), plus newer DL entrants: Yang et al. 2021 message-passing GNN on molecular graph geometry that recovers secondary-structure sensitivity without being explicitly told about it; Kang et al. 2020 weakly-supervised learning that uses unassigned-shift peak lists to augment training data; ExpNN-ff (Guan et al. 2021) SchNet-variant GNN trained first on DFT shifts then transfer-learned to experiment. For solids: ShiftML (2018 SOAP-kernel Gaussian process on 2500 CSD structures), ShiftML2 (2022 32-model kernel-ridge ensemble with SOAP on 16,013 CSD, extended to 12 elements and to MD snapshots — the direct methodological precedent for applying shift prediction along MD trajectories), 3D-DenseNet, IMPRESSION (kernel ridge with Coulomb-matrix / aSLATM / FCHL). The review is explicit that deep learning is not yet dominating protein shift prediction, and that learning directly on molecular-graph geometry (Yang 2021-style) offers a more generalisable path than handcrafted features.

Protein dynamics (Section 3.4) is directly relevant to our Stage 2: Wang et al. [110] predicts backbone ¹H-¹⁵N order parameter S² from NMR structure ensemble alone (random forest on Cα-distance variance, torsion variance, SASA), explicitly because order-parameter depositions are scarce — only 36 proteins were assemble-able. PDBCor [114] detects correlated motion from NMR ensembles via distance / torsion matrix per residue → GMM clustering → adjusted mutual information, without requiring structure superposition. Section 5.4 quantifies the data scarcity that shapes this whole field: although 14,500 NMR structures and similar numbers of chemical shift depositions exist, multi-dimensional time-domain or spectral data is in <1.5% of BMRB entries — which is why big-data ML cannot scale here and why novel-angle contributions are the route. ARTINA [6] trained on only 100 proteins [67] is remarkable precisely for this constraint. NMRPrime [135] is the recent database response.

Implications for our work.
