This review article surveys structurally oriented experimental datasets from nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and room-temperature (RT) protein crystallography for benchmarking protein force fields. Targeted at computational researchers developing or validating molecular dynamics simulations and machine learning ensembles, it argues that robust evaluation requires testing against peptides, folded proteins, and intrinsically disordered proteins. The authors categorize observables by experimental uncertainty, simulation convergence speed, structural scope (local versus tertiary), and consensus on calculation best practices, recommending a tiered benchmarking strategy that prioritizes low-uncertainty, fast-converging metrics.

The paper presents no new simulations or quantum calculations. It systematically reviews methodologies for computing NMR and crystallographic observables from trajectories. For NMR, it details empirical chemical shift predictors (SHIFTS, PROSHIFT, Camshift, SPARTA+, SHIFTX2, PPM, UCB-Shift, GraphNMR), the Karplus equation for scalar J-couplings, alignment tensor fitting for residual dipolar couplings (RDCs), effective distance models for nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE) spectroscopy, the Lipari-Szabo model-free approach for spin relaxation, and the Solomon-Bloembergen equation for paramagnetic relaxation enhancement (PRE). For crystallography, it covers Bragg diffraction intensity calculation, Debye-Waller (B-factor) computation, alternate conformation occupancy analysis, and diffuse scattering modeling. It outlines setup protocols for solvation, ion placement, and thermodynamic ensembles, and analysis protocols for equilibration, convergence, and statistical significance.

The review catalogs specific datasets with quantitative details. The Beauchamp dataset contains 524 chemical shifts and J-couplings for 31 systems. The Robustelli dataset spans 21 proteins/peptides with over 9,000 data points. The Stroet dataset includes 52 structures with 13 NMR-derived models. The Mao dataset comprises 41 folded proteins. Spin relaxation data cover approximately 350 proteins in the BMRB, with a curated set of 133 used to train the Dyna-1 deep learning model. Crystallographic datasets range from 0.65 Å to 1.74 Å resolution, including HEWL (PDB IDs 2LZT, 6O2H, 4LZT, 2VB1), Crambin (3U7T, 9EWK, R-factor 0.0591), CypA (4YUO), and PTP1B (6B8X). Regarding predictor performance, the SPARTA+ standard deviation for Cα shift predictions is 0.94 ppm on its training database; C′ chemical shift predictions for disordered proteins frequently achieve RMSDs below 0.5 ppm, compared to ~1 ppm for folded proteins. In HEWL crystal simulations, B-factors from ff14SB align more closely with experiment than those from CHARMM36, ff14ipq, or ff99SB.
