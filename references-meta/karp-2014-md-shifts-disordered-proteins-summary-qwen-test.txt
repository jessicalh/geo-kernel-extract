Karp, J. M., Erylimaz, E., & Cowburn, D. (2015). Correlation of chemical shifts predicted by molecular dynamics simulations for partially disordered proteins. Journal of Biomolecular NMR, 61, 35–45. DOI: 10.1007/s10858-014-9879-2. 11 pages. Supported by NIH R01 GM086868-16 (Tom Muir, PI), T32 GM007288-39, and XSEDE (ACI-1053575).

This research paper evaluates whether molecular dynamics (MD) simulation ensembles improve NMR chemical shift prediction accuracy over static crystal structures for partially intrinsically disordered proteins. The authors examine two split intein systems, Npu DnaE from Nostoc punctiforme (PDB ID: 2keq) and RadA from Pyrococcus horikoshii (PDB ID: 4e2t), which contain extended unstructured regions. The paper argues that standard MD sampling is insufficient for disordered segments, and that accelerated MD (aMD) provides a more representative conformational ensemble for chemical shift prediction. The study is positioned as a methodological assessment for computational biophysicists and NMR spectroscopists working with dynamic protein systems.

The authors performed standard MD and aMD simulations using the AMBER99sb force field and TIP3P water model. Standard MD ran for 200 ns with a 1 fs timestep, extracting coordinates every 5 ps to yield 40,270 frames. aMD simulations used boost parameters ED = 1,967 kcal/mol, EP = -78,656 kcal/mol, aD = 100, and aP = 5,372 for Npu, and ED = 2,425 kcal/mol, EP = -72,180 kcal/mol, aD = 122, and aP = 5,026 for RadA. Frames were selected by a kDVBT threshold (>110 for Npu, >90 for RadA), clustered using complete-linkage clustering (12 clusters for Npu, 20 for RadA), and the lowest-energy frame per cluster initiated a 1 ns standard MD run. Chemical shifts were predicted using SHIFTX2 version 1.07 and SPARTA? version 2.80. Conformational sampling was analyzed via principal component analysis on Cα coordinates, and prediction accuracy was quantified by root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) against experimental values.

For the Npu intein, SHIFTX2 RMSDs (ppm) were: Normal MD (1.30, 1.38, 3.29, 3.18, 0.60); aMD (1.18, 1.34, 3.23, 2.78, 0.54); NMR ensemble (1.18, 1.23, 3.28, 2.80, 0.54). SPARTA? RMSDs were: Normal MD (1.24, 1.28, 3.37, 3.39, 0.58); aMD (1.08, 1.28, 3.31, 3.08, 0.54); NMR (1.10, 1.18, 3.34, 3.14, 0.55). For the RadA intein, SHIFTX2 RMSDs were: X-ray (0.98, 1.08, 0.94, 2.89, 0.51); Normal MD (1.02, 1.14, 1.01, 3.29, 0.52); aMD (0.94, 1.10, 1.00, 2.97, 0.51); NMR (0.99, 1.09, 0.98, 3.04, 0.46). SPARTA? RMSDs were: X-ray (0.96, 1.04, 1.01, 3.09, 0.50); Normal MD (0.99, 1.17, 1.06, 3.24, 0.50); aMD (0.91, 1.10, 1.07, 3.01, 0.49); NMR (0.91, 1.06, 1.06, 3.03, 0.49). The paper concludes that aMD provides a modest improvement over standard MD, with predictions remaining comparable to static X-ray or NMR ensemble structures. For flexible C-terminal residues, the aMD boost potential flattens shallow potential wells, skewing predicted distributions away from experimental values. Prediction accuracy correlates spatially with local conformational sampling fidelity.

The paper builds on and contrasts with Lehtivarjo et al. (2012) and Robustelli et al. (2012), who reported that MD ensembles consistently improve shift prediction. It cites Han et al. (2011) and Shen and Bax (2010) for the prediction algorithms, and Markwick et al. (2010) and Shen and Hamelberg (2008) for aMD weighting considerations. The authors point toward Camilloni et al. (2012, 2013), Baskaran et al. (2010), and Stafford et al. (2013) regarding ensemble averaging and mutational perturbation, and note De Simone et al. (2009) and Tamiola et al. (2010) on sequence-based prediction for disordered proteins.

For the thesis, this paper demonstrates that static geometries and standard MD ensembles do not reliably capture the conformational distributions required for accurate chemical shift prediction in disordered regions. The finding that aMD better captures relevant ensemble features supports the thesis requirement for explicit conformational sampling in kernel calibration rather than reliance on single-structure inputs. The spatial correlation between sampling accuracy and prediction error aligns with the thesis approach of per-residue kernel weighting. However, the study quantifies error via RMSD rather than correlation metrics, collapses the shielding response to scalar chemical shifts, and does not address rank-2 tensor preservation or DFT-based calibration. Its primary utility for the project is establishing that geometric kernels must be robust to conformational heterogeneity, and that ensemble averaging should be treated as a signal-capture problem rather than a pointwise matching exercise.
